One concept I like to play with is the idea, and have come to actually believe is that societies can be modeled in such a way as to gain better understanding by applying traditional physics concepts.  The idea is that if we arrange the concepts in the right way, we could apply the laws of mechanics, either newtonian or relativistic, and eventually hopefully determine more stable configurations for our societies.

The question then is how to design a testable model.  How could you define space  with respect to a society?  Clearly society doesn't behave the same way in physical space as other physical matter, or we would all be at sea level.

However there are little parallels that pop up in some abstract ways.  For instance, in [this article from MIT's technology review](https://www.technologyreview.com/s/534251/astrophysicists-prove-that-cities-on-earth-grow-in-the-same-way-as-galaxies-in-space/), it is noted that there are scaling laws that show patterns in city development that are similar to galaxy development.  They both follow Zipf's law, which is a scaling distribution that also applies to language.  It basically says that the largest city tends to be N times bigger than the Nth biggest city (so twice as big as the second biggest city).  This tends to hold at small scales and larger scales.  So in a country, region, etc.  In this way it could point to some fractal geometry.

This doesn't just apply to things in physical space, though.  It also applies to the distribution of words in a language . In fact this is where the law was originally discovered.  There are many of these kinds of distributions that pop up in may different realms.  If we take "space" to mean just any dimension, or degree of freedom, then there are many mathematical tools that already exist for dealing with this.
