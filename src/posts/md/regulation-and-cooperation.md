So regulations benefit a portion of society. There are always people paying for the regulation and people benefitting from it. Sometimes, it's the same people. Other times, it's not. It would only make sense that if the cost of a regulation is lower than the benefit, we would not want it to be implemented. In this way, the decision to regulate is the same as the decision of whether to build infrastructure.

Both of them can be modeled as many-player prisoner's dilemma games. When public investment in this infrastructure or regulation is denied, then the situation falls to the tragedy of the commons. A private group will not fill in the gap until they have amassed enough capital, and can reasonably expect to find a way to make a return on that capital.

![](http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_wuSqJG5bIKE/TDh2qBpmOkI/AAAAAAAAAiU/ADiTlIKZSRA/s1600/Tragedy+of+the+Commons.030.png)

This is a basic explanation of a "prisoner's dilemma" game.  As you can see, the prisoners gain more in any given game if they defect while the other cooperates than if they both cooperate.  So, it is in their immediate interest in any given game to defect in case the other player tries to cooperate. 

In real life, many of these situations are better modeled as a game like this iterated many times.  In these cases, it's possible to learn the strategy of another player and make a decision about whether they will cooperate. Depending on the goal, this can lead to cooperation, or it can result in the same tragedy of the commons.  

One major difference is whether the goal is to get the maximum payout, versus winning.  If the goal is to win overall, then all you have to do is defect when they cooperate 1 time.  As long as you always defect, they will never catch up.  However, if the goal is to maximize payout, then it is possibly in the interest of both parties to cooperate in order to avoid getting stuck in the defection loop.
