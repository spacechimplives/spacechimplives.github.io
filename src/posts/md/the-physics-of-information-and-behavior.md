As I said in the last post, when you communicate through an ICF (institutional communication framework), different elements of information are captured and passed on.  Others are ignored.  This is the process of abstraction, but it is also key to the process of attention.

By filtering information, the institution limits data to something it can process and synthesize an institutional response to.  The distribution of information becomes critical to the behavior of the entity.

By sending information to different places internally, the institution can create different informational environments that encourage specialization.  This is often derided as "the silo effect," but it is also necessary for an institution to behave.

These are asymmetries.  Wherever things are happening, there are asymmetries.  There is some gradient across which things are flowing.  In order for work to be done consistently, this gradient must be actively maintained, like a hydrogen pump in mitochondria, or a sodium potassium pump, or a water tower.

There is a constant pressure against the gradient, but there are ways to pause that flow--particularly through containment.

The time it takes for information to be collected, analyzed, synthesized into a response, and behaved upon is proportional to the size of the institution.  From a very abstracted view this could be seen as something like inertia.  Could it be modeled as such?
