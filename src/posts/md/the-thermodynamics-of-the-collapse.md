We have the tools.

Joseph Tainter has a great explanation of what this actually looks like on the ground. For example, he describes Chaco canyon civilization and how they controlled a fresh water supply for the area. They would build cisterns that they would then use to exchange goods for water, reducing the distance between neighboring realms and the water. But as they built more cisterns, each one provided less and less in trade because they were competing with each other. The water was also physically more plentiful further away from the canyon, so the gradient of water asymmetry was less steep. As time went on the success of their operations diminished, but the society was organized around this process.. those with influence over decision-making and assets were those who controlled the cisterns, so decision-making was biased toward what worked for them, and they were incentivized to keep building cisterns. So they kept doing it to the neglect of other collective investments, and the society collapsed.

Tainter describes the historical arc of a society as running parallel to the marginal return on investment in complexity. In other words: given an amount of money (or energy if we want to describe things with physics) put into maintaining an organized process, how much money (or energy) is returned to the system which created the process. What Tainter points out is that the highest return items are more likely to be found first.. this means there's inherently a diminishing marginal yield for any process of energy acquisition.

Each of these energy acquisition processes is taking advantage of some potential difference. As work is done over that potential difference, the gradient decreases in steepness, so less work can be done across that gradient.

This has a parallel to the thermodynamics of life, for instance as described in "Energy and the Evolution of Life" by Ronald Fox, which he describes as the energetic ourobouros. The parallel to life is: work is done on the compositional units of life (atoms), which constrains their behavior (e.g. binds them in a molecule for a time, ionizes them, etc.), in turn causing them to constrain external units (e.g. preventing the diffusion of molecules being digested to outside the cell, photons during photosynthesis, amoebas trapping other organisms, etc.). A certain amount of energy is then collectively acquired as potential energy through releasing the potential energy from that external source (e.g. storing energy via kreb’s cycle). That released potential energy can then in turn be used to do work on internal units, which will then do work on external units, which will then release their potential energy into the system again.

If the ratio of energy expended to energy acquired falls below a certain threshold, the processes collapse.

In terms of society, that same ourobouros looks like this means work is done on each of the compositional units of society (people), which constrains their behavior (e.g. hires them as an employee, binds them with a contract, etc.). Those internal units then constrain external units (e.g. throwing a spear to kill a buffalo, constraining a plant to grow in a certain place, etc.). Energy is collected from that work (e.g. harvesting, storing grain, etc.) and stored as potential energy for redistribution (trading for money, bartering, etc.), and then used to harness more work from people, who can then continue the cycle.

If the ratio of energy expended constraining the behavior of individuals falls below the energy acquired from the process, the processes collapse.

As we can see, this is not some mysterious process hidden in the depths of intractable chaos. It’s a very present and preventable thing. We should be dumping money that rivals what we’re putting into AI into anything that can help us understand society and institutions ASAP. Any billionaire that pretends to care about society should be making this a priority. This includes game theory and category theory, mechanism design, cybernetic that’s getting a resurgence, etc. and combining it with thermodynamics to determine how to at least soften this collapse and perhaps turn it around.
