In a US presidential election, an infinitude of choices are boiled down to 2 candidates.  There are a few dichotomies that generally take center stage.

First, let's describe the apparent infinity of outcomes, and then see how they are systematically cancelled out from each other to result in finite abstract bits of input.

When people imagine how they want the future to be, their imaginations are abstractions, but not as abstract as language.  Most people envision a version of the US that is similar to what we have, but with less unemployment.  In their mind, an image might flash of an unemployment office with a clerk and a waiting room.  Their desire for the future is fewer people in that waiting room.  They imagine the people in the waiting room being happier.  Their mind populates those people with stock backgrounds and psychologies.  The jobs available are populated from their conception of prototypical jobs.  The content of their prototypical jobs arises from their past experiences.  It is generally not going to include details like a particular company that the job is with, let alone the earnings per share of such a company.  It is generally not going to include a particular profession, let alone details about the particular tools or training needed to do the job.  Through abstraction, all these details are somehow subtracted away, or averaged out into general shadows.  However, we know that in order for these jobs to actually exist in reality, these details need to be fleshed out.

A person imagining a shorter line at the unemployment office also often has opinions about how to achieve that.   Some people believe that the line will get shorter if companies have more money to hire more people.  Other people believe the line will get shorter if people have more money to start more companies.  These beliefs will generally be informed by experience.  The experience that a person has, or the stories they've heard about people getting jobs will give them examples of paths that have been taken from unemployment to employment.  Each experience that they had was rich in detail.  It was a particular job with a particular company.  A person whose experience in employment is based in a town where most people are truck drivers will have a very truck-driver biased view of employment.  They will have few examples of situations where low gas prices reduced the number of jobs available.  So, they might tend to generalize that low gas taxes create jobs.  This is an example where a detail is not subtracted.  Because their experience is steeped in a given context, the details of that context are not filtered out through the averaging of their experiences.

If they move away from that town, or hear stories from people with other experiences, the connection between gas prices and job creation will be weakened.  That path may exist, but there may be alternative paths as well, and a weight may be assigned to each path.  And that weight may be modulated by other details of the situation.  The formation of an intermediary branch between the premise and conclusion makes the correlation more abstract and allows contextual information to be addressed in the conclusions.  Most people have several steps at least involved in their thinking about topics like this.

For instance, most people agree that above a certain level, minimum wage laws hurt industry, and below a certain level, wages can be so low as to make an inefficient labor market.  Debates between people in a private setting generally end up as discussions about where to set that level.  However, if you zoom out and look at the debates on a political level you generally have a camp that advocates for a higher minimum wage, and a camp that advocates for a lower one, and the camps spar for media attention.   All the details of their reasons for thinking one way or another are ignored, or lost in the noise because they vary so much.  What you're left with is generally a pool of people who support a higher minimum wage because it is in their interest, and a pool who supports a lower one for the same reason.

In this way, the minimum wage has some mechanism for reaching an equilibrium.  It is biased towards those with more money, however it exists.  If it did not, there would be a feedback loop that forced it to 0 or infinity.  Each person who has an opinion either expresses it or doesn't, and that information filters through a variety of routes, but is ultimately integrated through the political system and then there is an output.

This abstract output from the political system then must be converted back in to reality.  It must trigger a physical response from the system.  The information is conveyed to the public, and then employers change the numbers in their spreadsheets.  Those who don't have action taken against them.  A cascade of institutional and human behaviors takes place.

In a referendum, this would basically be the end of the story.

However, in the US political system, we vote for candidates.  Upon each candidate is assigned a set of stances and a priority to each stance.  So in this minimum wage example, rather than simply two groups hashing out the minimum wage law, they will have to support a candidate who supports their view.  This means someone who wants a higher minimum wage will support a candidate who supports a higher minimum wage.   Let's say the minimum wage is $5 and a voter wants the minimum wage at $10, for their variety of theoretical and contextual reasons.  Suppose there are 2 candidates, one who supports a minimum wage of $20 and another who doesn't support any change in the minimum wage.  The person who wants the minimum wage at $10 will tend to support the $20 candidate.  The result is that the choices tend to align to unconditional dichotomies.  A voter's actual views are not represented, only a general indicator: up or down.  You end up voting for someone who wants a higher wage or a lower wage, but rarely someone who wants a wage that is set relative to the GDP PPP per capita.

Not only that, but multiple of these dichotomous viewpoints are attached to each single candidate.  So you end up with something like this:

In this way, the political system removes all of these intermediary steps from a premise to a conclusion.  There is no way of incorporating a system of logic in the political system which correlates lower gas prices with more jobs under certain conditions and fewer jobs under other conditions.  Even though it is theoretically possible, the process of opposition of the 2 candidates filters out the internal logic of any argument and hollows it out into its inputs and outputs.  This is bad in a lot of ways, but representative in some ways.  A person generally changes their logical framework to match their interests.  A person who suddenly becomes poor will have experiences which lead them to support a higher minimum wage.  A person who suddenly becomes rich will tend to have experiences leading them to support lower minimum wages.  This will cause a reorganization of their logical frameworks and different assessments of the inputs.

You can imagine something similar happening in the body.  The body has the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous responses to certain stimuli.  Groups of actions are taken.  The information sent to the brain is not a complex set of instructions, but simple bits of information.  One major difference is that in the body, these responses are triggered at thresholds.  The everyday function of the body is not locked into this dichotomous simplification of instruction.

Since the more complex ideas of people are filtered into simple inputs through the political abstraction process, the complexity that is added back in through the behavioral cascade is not always appropriately modulated based on the context.
