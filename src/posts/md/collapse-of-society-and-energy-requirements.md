So if we’ve properly established that [agreements are mutual constraints, which are an increase in internal energy](https://spacechimplives.substack.com/p/mutual-constraint-as-internal-energy), then we have to determine where that energy came from. Conservation of energy dictates that there must be some energy going into the system to fuel these agreements. As Joseph Tainter points out in _The Collapse of Complex Society_, the agreements that combine to make up a society are a form of complexity. In order for people to engage in all these agreements and business, there must be some return on their investment in that complexity. Otherwise it would be quickly abandoned. This return on investment in complexity is then distributed among the members of society. The energy is then stored in equipment, and the internal energy of said equipment, as well as the internal energy of individuals or agents who can fulfill the roles outlined in the agreements. First let’s talk about what counts toward an increase in that internal energy. In our case, let’s hypothesize that transactions are the dominant form of agreement.

This doesn’t have to be the case. So let’s justify it for a bit. We can see that 2 tigers who mutually constrain each other’s behavior by defending incursions into their respective territory would fall into this model of mutual constraint of behavior, and so would constitute an increase in internal energy. However we can also see that humans consistently choose to follow their financial incentives over whatever that constraint on behavior is. Otherwise there would still be significantly many wild areas where a modern economy hasn’t dominated because people find the violence to be a stronger deterrent than monetary gain is an incentive.

We can also say that the dominant form of agreement that humans make is transactions. Nowadays subscriptions are more and more common, but those are a category of transaction. And I don’t think it would be too controversial to say that the number of financial transactions we make per day far outweighs the number of contracts for other things like real estate, etc… probably by several orders of magnitude. Perhaps more complex contracts account for larger value agreements on average, but I think we can assume that the volume of financial transactions outweighs that difference.

Now that energy to make and fulfill those transactions has to come from somewhere. While we get a lot of our energy from food, it doesn’t tip the scales much compared to how much we consume in the form of electricity and gas. So we should be able to find some correlations between transaction volume and electricity consumption, mediated through the internal energy.
