Morality is a touchy subject that science doesn't like to address.  However, for all the criticism science levels at religion, it has offered no real alternative framework.

There have been scientific attempts to make the primary imperative to act in the interest of the survival of the human species, and through this, attempts to explore empathy.  But rarely does it offer any sort of input on the sorts of lifestyle dilemmas that religion addresses.  In fact, it is often used as a justification for horrible things, such as maiming kittens, the holocaust, tuskegee syphilis experiments, etc.  While it is absurd to deny natural selection, the genetic components of behavior are often vastly over-stated, and a limited understanding of these things are used to rationalize obviously unethical behavior and cynical worldviews.

A concept that I have never seen addressed is the scale of morality.  Full-on moral relativism seems a little nihilistic to me, because there are some things that are clearly considered wrong across cultures.  Killing someone simply because you don't like them seems to be unacceptable in all cultures.  You at least have to make up an excuse.  I have never come across a culture where eating your own children was considered a good thing. There seems to be a preference for certain actions over others among all humans, regardless of a full understanding of the consequences.  A boss mistreating his workers is almost never portrayed as a good character trait in any narrative.  A lot of moral commonalities seem to surround treatment of those with less power.

But there is also clearly moral grey area.  I propose that morality is overly-abstracted.  Dealing drugs is bad.  But dealing drugs because you don't know of another way to help your family out of debt slavery is viewed as morally OK by many people.  Killing is bad.  But killing someone who raped your daughter?  Many people find that acceptable.  What happening is simply a conflict.  You have a duty to your family, and a duty to society, and a duty to your employer, and a duty to your book club.  All of these things weigh into decisions, and ultimately what you prioritize defines your values.

So, the workaholic who puts in 80 hours a week and never goes to his kids baseball games gets promoted because he is doing his duty to his employer.  The kid who is hanging out with the wrong crowd instead of doing his homework is aligning himself with that group of people in their ad-hoc institution.  Each person has a limited amount of attention to give, and where they focus that attention is how they align themselves.

Now, Marxism is an attempt to prioritize lower-scale morality.  It emphasizes the needs of individual humans over institutions, and does not define processes for allowing the higher-level institutions to exist.  This is all fine and good, and makes people happier, but it is unsustainable because a system which promotes a focus on higher-scale morality will breed more effective institutions, and eventually, it will be able to overtake such a system.  Not only that, but institutions are inherent to humans with language.  So even without outside influence, institutions will form in the cracks where policy did not dictate decision-making processes.

Capitalism prioritizes higher-scale morality to institutions.  This makes for a more resilient system because it can digest larger tasks.  The invisible hand does a good job of maximizing the effectiveness of institutions and rewarding productiveness.  But it runs the risk of forcing people to focus too little on the human scale that life has such little reward that they're willing to die or live in anarchy for the hope that life will be more enjoyable for the people they care about, or that they can experience a more enjoyable life after death.  This clearly is not an effective system either.

The two (or more) scales of morality need to align.  How do you do this?

Part of it has to do with information flow.  A system must have a balance where people can give as much information to institutions as they give to people.  Smaller corporations intuitively "understand" this.  They have very short lifespans for the most part, and their existence depends not only on the amount, but also the quality of the work they extract from their employees.  Angry employees don't make good judgement calls, and smaller corporations leave a lot to judgement calls.  So a lot of smaller corporations have a lot of information flowing back and forth between the ground level workers and the CEO.  Furthermore, that distance is just smaller for small corporations.

A larger corporation tends to have less information flow.  Yet they tend to define more rigid policies.  This actually goes hand-in-hand.  When a person has less say in the tasks they are performing, they are less likely to be motivated to perform them.  This is because they are more likely to run into a task that they think should not be done, or at least is not necessary, and partially because they intuitively foresee that increased likelihood.  Even if the motivation were not a factor, employees in this situation would perform less effectively, because if they don't understand why a task is being done, their human judgement calls are more likely to err.

And, no matter how many policies a company defines, these judgement calls are what make or break the effectiveness of the system.  You can't define everything in policy, no matter how detailed.  And, even if you could, at that point, you would be better served to give those instructions to robots.  If you defined them rigorously enough to remove the significance of human judgement, it would be to such detail that a robot could perform it, anyway.

So in order for an institution to thrive, it must align the morality of its members with its own morality.  This means that the members must have a say in the tasks they are performing.  For this to happen, there has to be an institutional framework for determining the tasks they find appropriate.

In the economy and large corporations, this is done through the labor market.  People traditionally have some choice over their employer,.

In society, this is done through the government.  People traditionally have some choice over their leaders.  Whether it is through the game-theory dynamics stated in previous posts, or through democracy.

In small-level work groups and teams, this is done through language.  People give suggestions to the person in charge on the best way to get something done.

But the interaction between lower-scale morality and higher-scale morality is very complex.  A person will take a job doing something that they believe is bad for society because it is good for their family.  If they employ their friends and there is a conflict between the needs of the institution and the needs of their friends, it will create a significant conflict.  In these cases, the narrative that comes from the institution will be different from the narrative that comes from their friend.  The decisions people make in cases like these end up defining their identity.  Each decision like this is an investment.

If they don't fire their friend, the institution is legitimately worse off.  But if they fire their friend, the friend is legitimately worse off.

Community is about lifting people up even when they make mistakes.  Humans can forgive.  Institutions don't so much, because they haven't developed that capacity.  It is considered too risky.  As humans invest more and more of their attention in higher-level institutions, they also tend to become more risk-averse and spend less attention on local morality.

Look at the prison system.  Once you go through it, you are essentially written off as unemployable.  If you robbed a store to buy a computer for your little brother so he could have an easier time in school, employers don't have any process for taking that into account, but individual humans do.

Take Barack Obama and Bill Ayers.  Obama has repeatedly denied the reality of their friendship.  I'm by no means contradicting that.  But imagine that they really were friends, and you were Bill Ayers?  How shitty would that feel?  Yet imagine you were Obama.  Either way, you're kind of screwed.

But think of the implications of that.  Obama had to do that to appeal to a wider range of people for the presidential campaign, but do you think he had to do that for his state senate campaign, too?  To someone maybe?  What about when he was studying to go to law school?  How many times did he probably have to tell his friends "no" about hanging out?  What if one of them had something they really needed to talk about and he wasn't there to help them out of it?  Is this good?  It's certainly not the worst thing, but it's the opposite of building community.

If that is what is required to be successful in an institution, then people will slowly align themselves more and more with that behavior, and eventually the policies they dictate will be more and more at odds with those who do value community, and they will be forced to abandon it.

Over-federalization tends to do this.  Of course federalization also allows for larger public investments, so it's a trade-off.  But that dynamic exists and should be taken into account explicitly.
