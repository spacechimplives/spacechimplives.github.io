So when we look at business processes as institutional code, we have to take a deeper look into what exactly an institutional code is and what it does.  The most obvious example is law or business processes.

In this case we have a set of direct instructions that are executed under different circumstances. It all relies on humans to properly interpret and execute these instructions and the circumstances which trigger them.  This is done through incentives or enforcement.  Incentives are essentially a form of positive feedback and enforcement would be a form of negative feedback.  The probability of certain behaviors are changed by those mechanisms.

These enforcement/incentive mechanisms themselves require constraint of behavior somehow.  This circularity is reminiscent of the circularity in information accumulation in organisms whereby information from sensory receptors--as well as some actuation mechanisms in response to stimulus--is required to allow an organism to accumulate energy, but also that energy is required to maintain the sensory receptors and actuation mechanism.  A similar circularity exists in the requirement for energy in building the fundamental building blocks of life in what Ronald F Fox calls the "primitive uroborous" in Energy and the Evolution of Life.

In that chapter he also states that the equilibrium state in contact with a thermal reservoir is the state with minimum free energy, not maximum disorder.  This brings to mind Friston's Free Energy Principle, which treats free energy from an information theoretic point of view rather than the thermodynamic point of view that Fox was using, however [there is certainly some relationship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_in_thermodynamics_and_information_theory).   Digging into that relationship is part of the meat of this problem.  He describes the system that primordial life would have started in as "thermally buffered."  Fox says, "Energy flowing into a thermally buffered system drives the system away from its equilibrium state, which means that its free energy increases."  This makes sense... if the equilibrium state is minimum free energy and then energy flow into the system, then it is no longer at equilibrium.  He's essentially describing a dissipative system in the 80's before it became a buzzword.

The consequence of the free energy increasing is one of two things must happen: either entropy, or internal energy.  In the case of life, it was the latter which increased.  Specifically in the form of intra-molecular bonds.  These systems  would activate monomers to become primed to polymerize.
