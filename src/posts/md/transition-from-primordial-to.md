This video asks the question: why did human choose agriculture?  It makes the case that it was harder for the individuals.  They had to work longer hours, and their nutrition actually decreased.  They were exposed to more disease as well, due to pests etc.

https://youtu.be/b90oSidoHUY?si=5GkKXFiP8bZ-94wT&t=928

To explain this without assuming institutions can have their own intentionality, you have to rely on arbitrary explanations of circumstance.  For instance in the video, he suggests it was hypothesized to have been driven by climate changes, or another which seems more tenuous: that people gathered together to increase their status.  However once you accept that institutions do have their own intentionality which they are willing to sacrifice humans to fulfill, it becomes much easier to explain.

The time period in which this occurred was a clear shift in the nature of these institutions from their primordial forms to something in which the individual appears to have lost a significant amount of autonomy.

 

A later video by another youtuber makes a similar claim -- that technological innovation is essentially determined by cultural or various circumstantial factors.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E5HDQUuI6_E&ab_channel=toldinstone

While this is unarguably true on some level, I would argue that those cultural factors are a reflection of the circumstantial, and that the circumstantial factors matter to the extent that they affect the amount of wealth/potential energy which can be rationally allocated toward a collective interest.

To explain this a bit further:  imagine 20 people have shops along a dirt road.  Imagine the road gets washed out and nobody can use it.  If I can't use the road, and that's my only source of income, I'm incentivized to fix it myself, even if nobody pitches in.

However fixing it costs me X per week on average.  Let's put that at $10.  And let's say that it costs $10,000 to pave the road.  That means it takes about 20 years to pay itself off.  If I have other things I need that $10,000 for, then it's not worth it to me to pave the road.  However if I can convince my 19 neighbors to each pitch in $500, then it will pay itself off in more like a year.  Then it may be worth it.

Unfortunately if one decides to back out, they still get the benefits.  If there are no consequences to them backing out, they can do so and be better off.  That applies to everyone, so everyone will back out.  This means that in the absence of an enforcement mechanism, the quickest way for the road to get paved would be for one person to become very rich, so that $10,000 is easy enough for them to spend that they just do it.

If we assume that you I need 20x my current wealth to make this worthwhile, and I increase my wealth by ~5% /yr it will take me ~60 years before it's worth it for me to build that road.  If the neighbors are all experiencing that same $10/wk cost, then that means it's costing $200 /wk.  $200/wk * 60 years is over $600k dollars.  So there's a strong incentive to create the enforcement mechanism to allow this pooling of wealth for collective activity.

Instead of financial terms, we can look at this through the lens of thermodynamics.  The enforcement mechanism is essentially a type of bond.  A reaction becomes spontaneous when it results in a decrease in Gibbs Free Energy.  This means there's either a decrease in the amount of energy produced, an increase in temperature, or an increase in the entropy.
