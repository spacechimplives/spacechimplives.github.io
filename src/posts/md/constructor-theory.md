I was just introduced to "[Constructor Theory](http://constructortheory.org/index.html)" today.., which is an attempt at reformulating the laws of physics only by describing what is possible and what is impossible.  It describes itself like this:

> #### The basic principle of constructor theory is that all fundamental laws of nature are expressible entirely in terms of statements of which tasks (i.e. classes of physical transformations) are possible and which are impossible, and why. This is a new mode of explanation, intended to supersede the prevailing conception of fundamental physics which seeks to explain the world in terms of its state (describing everything that is there) and laws of motion (describing how the everything changes with time). By regarding counter-factuals ('X is possible' or 'X is impossible') as first-class, exact statements, constructor theory brings all sorts of interesting fields, currently regarded as inherently approximative, potentially into fundamental physics. These include the theories of information, knowledge, thermodynamics, life, and of course the universal constructor.

It mirrors closely with some things which Terrence Deacon (and possibly those he plagiarzied: Thompson and Juarrero--I have yet to read the source material) calls for dealing with counterfactuals as "constrained degrees of freedom," and creating models which allow for them to have consequences.

The basic idea behind this theory is that physical events are transformations of substrates by constructors.  So a substrate has "attributes" which are modified by the constructor.  This seems like it will run into problems with conflating substrate and attribute. However I can imagine a starting point that is so flexible while having the potential descriptive power constructor theory is claimed to have.

Departing from there, comes the second principle:

> II. There exists a mode of description such that the state of the combined system S1 ⊕S2 of any two substrates S1 and S2 is the ordered pair (x,y) of the states x of S1 and y of S2 , and any construction undergone by S1 and not S2 can change only x and not y.

This helps elucidate my hesitation with the initial foundation.  This is claimed to be basically a restatement of Einstein's principle of locality, which is considered necessary for treating systems as quasi-enclosed, which is in turn deemed necessary for formulating laws of physics.  That's all fine and good as long as it is acknowledged as a heuristic and a limit to what we're willing to take into account.  Of course we have to ignore some things to make any meaningful statements, but I personally think a complementary system should always be taken which takes the opposite assumption and ignores something else instead.

There needs to be fundamental assumptions to form the basis of abstractions, but you can simultaneously create a system with other fundamental assumptions, abstract through that system, and then integrate both views, though it may be impossible to do so systematically.

 

Here are some things I'm looking for at the outset, though: Why not treat the concept of "possible" and "impossible" as continuous likelihoods, rather than discrete. This is addressed in stating that statistical descriptions are "at most descriptions at an emergent level."  This at least does not rule out the possibility that probabilities can be used to determine whether something is possible or not.

This term could be viewed as a "statistical distance" from our universe.  Anything that is impossible could be considered "infinitely statistically distant" from our universe, and our job would be to make sure there's a 0 in the denominator for that probability for things that are impossible.

It could be described in terms of the work required to make them real.  Travelling over the speed of light would require infinite work, so it's not going to be possible, so the probability term should go to 0.

It could be described in terms of the number of degrees of freedom that are different from reality.  If you're looking at 2 cars that are different colors but the same model, they differ only in a few degrees of freedom.  If you're looking at a bus and a car, it's more.  If you're looking at a tree and a car, there is very little similarity, and you have to dig to the level of the atomic structure to find symmetries.  The differences between our universe and one with different laws of physics will have even fewer or no symmetries.

By adding a quantity to the degree of truth that something has, you can really take advantage of the added structure of these counterfactuals.

Another thing that will have to be dealt with is inheritance.   In a given system, what is possible and impossible will change.
