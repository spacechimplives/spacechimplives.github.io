I believe that the winning strategy in the country is totally different from the winning strategy in the city.  That isn't the only divide but it's a major one.  In the city, specialization leads to maximizing revenue as a primary strategy, whereas in the country, minimizing costs by doing things oneself and maintaining independence are the best economic strategies.  This leads to fundamentally different goals in many areas of life.

I think this merits a little bit more explanation.  First of "city" and "country" are obviously an arbitrary difference.  The two strategies aren't totally mutually exclusive, and either strategy can exist in either place.  When I say "city" i just mean a place with relatively higher population density.  And the "country" is a place with relatively lower population density.

But what I mean is that in the city, there are many people.  It has been widely acknowledged that cities promote specialization.  From an economic perspective, there are more potential customers even for a niche product in the city.  This should be self evident.  In NYC, there are more people in a reasonable distance looking for an Arabic tutor than in rural Arizona.

The transaction costs associated with driving very far, wasted time in meeting up, unpaid work time, difficulty of communication, etc. mean that to make money in the country, you're better off filling multiple roles to customers nearby, accepting the lower pay of not being a "premium niche service." From a buying perspective, when you buy a good or service in the country, a higher percentage of it goes to transportation and other transaction costs that are not really internal to the value of the item.  So, at least in comparison to the city, you're better off in the country being a generalist and taking care of your own needs by yourself--or with the help of some friends if they have some time--as much as possible.  The upshot is that your rent is lower there, so it is feasible for someone to do that.

In the city, on the other hand, people have less space to do things for themselves.  They can't build a workshop.  Also, they have to pay more for their rent, so they need to fetch a higher price for their services.   In the city, a business is more likely to run into the problem of having too many customers.  If there is someone who likes tutoring, they're better off picking a specialty that they enjoy well enough and becoming an expert so they can charge more for the niche service.  If there are more than enough customers to fill 8 hours a day, then you're better off finding a way to charge customers more than to find a way to get more customers.

In the city when a business has too many customers, they can afford to be brusque, like the soup nazi in Seinfeld.  In the country, if you don't make an effort to get along with those in your vicinity, you might have trouble getting business.

This means everything for policy.  First, even if infrastructure were evenly distributed between the city and the country, people in the country would generally have less use for it if they tend to do more things themselves.  So of course they're not going to want to pay taxes on it.

Second, the kinds of jobs that are acceptable to the two groups are also vastly different.  A minimum wage job might allow people to save up in some parts out in the country if the family owns their property, whereas it will net to a negative income in the city.

There are also different styles of communication that stem from those two strategies.  Someone trying to cut costs might not trust a company which spent a lot of money on advertising, because that money could have gone into the product, or into a cheaper price.  Whereas someone in the city might not trust a company that spent no money on marketing because it seems like they have no skin in the game and they aren't willing to bet on their expertise.  I'm sure people can draw parallels to how these personalities come out in real life.

Another thing is that the "city" strategy can exist in the country.  For example, large farms might operate that way.  They borrow money for equipment and to acquire more land to maximize revenue.  Similarly in the city, there probably are plenty of people who follow the cost-saving strategy.  For instance, in some industries there is extreme job security, so people accept lower pay for that security and attempt to live cheaply.

Without strategies in place to deal with these differences, we will continue having the right dump a bunch of unacceptable policies onto the city, and visa versa.
