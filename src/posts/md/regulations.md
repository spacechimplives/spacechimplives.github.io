It is possible for a regulation to be required in one context but not another. For instance, guns are much less useful in the city, and amount to a bigger public safety issue there as well. In the country, however, they serve the purpose of farming, and their utility in terms of self-defense is higher, too, since police are potentially further away. In a place with a high population density, though, they do not serve any hunting purpose (unless transported outside that high-density area), and their use as a security measure is diminished by the increased risk they pose.

Those are different spatial contexts. Laws can also have different utilities in different temporal contexts. Obviously many laws regarding telecommunications no longer make sense, because the technologies are different. Laws can also be intermittently useful. For instance, curfew laws can be declared that are only used in emergency situations. Or [here](https://phys.org/news/2016-11-game-theory-tragedies-commons-averted.html), where periodic drought causes the need for regulations in usage of water that are otherwise unnecessary.

The way that laws are traditionally applied to various contexts is to have national laws that are the least stringent, and then leave the details up to the states, who leave more of the details up to the localities. However this does not leave room to make a law that for example applied to all cities between the populations of 1 million and 3 million. Surely there are individual laws that do that sort of thing, but it's not the standard.

And if it is the case that a law applies only to cities with populations between 1 and 3 million, how much say should other cities get in that policy?

Another question that I wonder about is who should pay for the enforcement of regulations?
