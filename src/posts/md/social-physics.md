A goal cannot exist longer than it persists in some form of storage.  A behavior has some limit to its consistency.  A person getting tired at work doesn't just segfault like a computer... they just work with less and less precision.  Precision takes power.

Relatedly in society, as we have continued to expand and consume more and more power, we have also refined the resolution of our observations.  We can now observe large bright objects up to 10^26 meters away, and we can observe the effects of tiny neutrinos with a radius of 10^-24 meters.  Why are those orders of magnitude so close to the inverse of one another?  As we grow in size and power consumption, we are able to observe the universe in higher resolution.

The process of observation is similar for both very large and very small things. Finding a signal out of the noise that comes through a telescope is a similar statistical process in a lot of ways to finding the signal from a noise of many trials in a particle detector.  In both of them, you're essentially looking for something constant while everything else around it varies.

You may only be receiving a sample of 1 photon per minute from the star, so you have to be sure you're holding the telescope very very still, and you need to wait for a long exposure.  For the small thing, you need to run many high-powered trials.  At work here are time and energy.

So on some level precision requires work.  In order for information to be obtained with a high level of confidence, it takes energy.  Similarly, to act with a high level of precision, it takes energy.  When a person is tired, they cannot translate their goal into behavior with as much consistency.

When an observation is made, it produces information.  However, previous information can aid in the observation.  For instance, if we are trying to observe the moon precisely and we have information about its anticipated path, we can use that to track its path and thus observe it for longer and collect more photons that have bounced from its surface.  If we're trying to observe the behavior of a quark in a certain circumstance, our knowledge about what other particles can exist will help us determine which blip on the particle detector is the one we're trying to focus on.

So information can be use in the process of observation, and it is also the product of it.  Information is also used in action.  This is more clear.  Any goal will have some sort of informational representation in our mind, and there will be a threshold of precision beyond which we will consider the goal achieved.

The degree of precision determines the energy or work required to achieve the goal.  More information allows a higher degree of precision.  How?

In the performance of the action, minuscule corrections are needed.  When a singer is trying to reach a note, they don't do so immediately.  There will always be some slight variation and correction.  They will never reach the exact pitch they are looking for, they will slightly oscillate around it, or slide thorough it gradually.  Ideally, that oscillation or slide will be imperceptible (unless it is on purpose).  In order for that to be so, it will have to adjust quickly to any improper trajectory.  Like trying to parallel park, there is a window of not cutting hard enough and cutting too hard that you're trying to hit.  Each correction requires physical work.  Energy is put into changing the trajectory of atoms.

A more precise action will generally require more corrections, and thus more work.  However there is another effect at play.  Prior information can reduce the work required.  An expert requires fewer corrections to reach the same level of precision.  A telescope which follows the moon's orbits requires either less time or less energy (less energy\*time) to observe it to the same level of consistency.  And the more quantities you can anticipate about a particle you are trying to observe, the fewer trials (aka, time and/or energy) will be required to properly identify it out of the data, and create new information about it, i.e. extract the signal from the noise.

As you create this information, you gain a value of confidence in it.  The less error in your information, the stronger its effects will tend to be.

So more energy leads to more precision.  Is this because without energy the information is somehow corrupted, or because it is translated less faithfully?  I would say the latter.

More information leads to more precision.  More trials lead to more information.  Trials require energy.  So this is another path by which energy is translated into precision.

So in expending energy toward a goal, the work required will be some function of the physical work required as well as the trials/observations required to obtain the information that will be necessary to obtain in order to achieve the desired level of precision.
