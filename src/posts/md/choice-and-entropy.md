I propose that more choices is the same as more degrees of freedom, meaning higher entropy in a very real way.

A hydrogen atom floating along in space has no constraints on its movement in 3 dimensions, but it does constrain the kinds of dynamics it can exhibit.  It will move linearly until it hits another atom, exhibiting that billiard-ball physics and Brownian motion.

A hydrogen atom that is part of a DNA molecule doesn't have the immediate freedom, yet over time it has the potential to exhibit more complex patterns of behavior.

A freelance programmer has no immediate constraints on the kinds of work they can do, but they will not have access to the kinds of clients required for certain kinds of larger projects.  A programmer at a company can't work in their underwear and they might have to refrain from saying things to a boss, but they will have more opportunity to learn certain skills.

So the question is: are those different kinds of behavior really physical 'degrees of freedom'?

If so, it kind of makes sense that as time goes on, entropy increases, because as time goes on, some molecule somewhere will have exhibited a pattern that no other molecule has, creating a new 'degree of freedom' or pattern of existence.

Could these be modeled as fractal dimensions?  So that any hydrogen has a position on the axis of "being part of a DNA molecule," which can be heuristically modeled with discrete values.

Then the challenge would be to approximate positions along axes of huge swaths at once.  What about the "being part of a human" axis?   What effects does this have on the behavior?  What lower level degress of freedom are suppressed?

Not only that, but we would have to begin to define which of these dimensions apply to which elements.  A human can exhibit the "CEO" degree of freedom, but it is highly unlikely for a dog, much less a hydrogen atom to exhibit that.

There are an infinite number of paths a hydrogen atom can take to get from point A to point B.  Being part of a water molecule that then moves from point A to point B is one of them.

We will never be able to account for every path, but we can use heuristics to model the space.
