[This](http://www.brainpickings.org/2013/10/17/ok-plateau/) article hit close to home for me.  I have hit the "OK Plateau" on many subjects and turned to something else.  But, I think there is more information to be shared about this.  I have some half-assed theories.

One thing I hate about science, especially pre 90's psychology is the tendency to put things into defined stages or groups.  The cognitive=>associative=>autonomous progression seems very unintuitive to me.  All of these stages almost certainly feed into each other and coexist within a given skill-set.

Learning is turning a conscious process subconscious, making room so you can pay attention to something else.  If you're not consciously thinking about something, you're not going to come up with new processes, but simply modulate the amplitude of already-known processes.

Take driving home from work for example.  When you first start a job, you have to think about what streets you're going to take and plan them out.  Maybe you accidentally take a wrong turn the first few times.  The next time, you anticipate it.  And you keep doing that until you don't have to think about it.

The cognitive stage is when you're still picturing the map or reciting the directions in your head.  The associative stage is when you start putting [chunked](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chunking_(psychology)) parts of the path in sequence.  The autonomous stage is when you don't think about it.

However, you get pulled out of the autonomous stage often.  When you need to take a detour, for instance, you have to go back to the cognitive stage for a second.

Another thing worth noting is precision.  You focus on the tolerances that are relevant to the task.  So, you have to maintain relatively precise movement of your arms, and be sensitive to the pressure on the gas.  Does it matter what clothes you're wearing?  No.  So you rarely pay attention to that as a part of the task of driving.  If you're going to an interview, obviously, it's a different story.

The details of the things you aren't paying attention to tend to get more random.  They expand to their acceptable tolerance and then you address them.  If you're going down a road with wide lanes, you're going to wait a little longer to correct trajectory than if you're going down one with narrow lanes.

Where driving home differs from being, say, a musician, is motivation.  Motivation drives attention, which drives behavior.  When you're driving home, it is sufficient that you arrive at your destination without running into anything.  But if you want to be an expert musician, "sufficient" has a much different threshold.

The vast, vast majority of tasks we encounter have a threshold of sufficiency that is well-defined.  You either ate lunch or you didn't.  It isn't a competition.  Did you put your shirt on?  How well did you do it?  Of course this sounds absurd, and you would be tempted to say, "you can only be so good at putting a shirt on."  But that's not true. Any task has an infinite amount of variability to it.  Ask a military cadet after inspection how well he/she put his/her shirt on, and the question might not sound so strange.  The same goes for driving home.  If you put conscious thought into determining the likelihood of someone taking a left along a certain stretch of road, you can switch lanes and improve your commute performance.

Some tasks have essentially infinite thresholds of sufficiency.  Art, business strategy, sports performance, programming--just to name a few.  These are the realms in which we compete.  If you're Usain Bolt, you can say that you've reached the threshold of sprinting performance sufficiency.  Otherwise, you've got work to keep doing.  But in order to even get to that point, Usain Bolt must have had something else driving him besides other people's recognition.

This brings me to the first point of this essay.  The difference between an expert and an amateur is where they get their motivation.  It has to do with the dynamics of competition.

When you're a novice in a given field, or a child (a novice at everything), people tend to offer you support and lots of positive feedback.  If you're a writer, and a writing student seeks out your advice or criticism, you're likely to offer almost entirely positive feedback.  As someone gains skill in a field, they come into more intense direct competition with the existing task-performers.  At a certain point, most of the positive feedback drops out.  In life, it's called becoming an adult.  In writing novels, I'd imagine it's getting published.

When you're not trying to get published, you're no threat.  But once you make the rite of passage into the arena of competition, people generally aren't all that nice unless they want something from you or you're taking specialized roles on a team.

This is competition, which, thankfully, does not encompass all of human existence.  It is generally confined to standardized arenas with certain rituals, rules and jargon... often an entire subculture.

To get beyond the mass competition, you must find motivation outside of the approval of your peers.  People have a tendency to be defensive and insecure.  Unless they are generally accepted as being at the top of their field, the competition is fierce.  At the top, there tend to be more friendly rivalries.

After a certain point, the renown of being at the top of a field is enough to provide a certain level of validation, but in the spaces in between, that validation does not exist.  So, you must bring your own motivation (BYOM).

In a standard employment setting, this usually comes from money.  People have different sensitivities to money as a motivator.  It seems like it's usually deeply tied to what they lacked as children, or what their parents over-did.

Some people are driven by things other than money: approval from a loved one, approval from society, desire to avoid certain traumas, moral idealism, and on and on.

But, positive feedback as a primary motivation is not conducive to achieving status in a competitive field.  It might be good for cooperative fields, but a chess grandmaster will not get that way by being concerned with the feelings and opinions of other chess players.

One thing that is commonly cited as a benefit for achievement is being a little crazy.  People say that, but obviously there are some kinds of crazy that are disadvantageous.  The religious and pseudo-religious are often the most productive, because their motivation comes from moral idealism.

It's hard in this world to maintain a feeling of moral idealism in most jobs.  Most artists I know think art is the route to save the world.  If they can just communicate these certain concepts effectively enough, the world will change for the better.  Same with scientists.  Sometimes it's like "If I just explore this concept, it will change everything."  Sometimes it's "Science has given us so many good things.  If I do work hard at science, it will be beneficial."

Some fields aren't as amenable to that kind of idealization.  People who are into solving rubick's cubes at high speed aren't interested in its effect on humanity or getting money, but must have some underlying need to prove their intelligence.  I don't mean to belittle this, but to elucidate it. Understanding your root motivations allows you to put yourself in a spot where you can capitalize on them and feel more fulfilled.  They don't go away just because you identify them, but you can focus them.

So, the first point is about motivation.  The second has to do with attention.

In any task, there are different aspects that require different levels of precision.  In a field with an infinite threshold of sufficiency, where do you focus?  Most people pay their attention to the elements that people before them did.  This will give you attention in the short term, because presumably, those who set the standard are at the top of the field, and spitting their work back to them strokes their ego.  However, there are always opportunities to take a different route.  They call it "out of the box" thinking, but that's horribly cliched and doesn't explain it.

What it really is, is focusing on areas that most of the competition aren't focusing on.  In order to have the free attention to do that, you must practice the standard elements with extreme precision.  If you can automate a higher threshold of sufficiency, then you can trust that when you focus your attention elsewhere, the automated details will still not wander outside the threshold.
