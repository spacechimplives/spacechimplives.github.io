While I have emphasized that mutual behavioral constraint is necessary for higher-level organization of society, I should also emphasize that it is responsible for institutional oppression.  The process by which laws are made varies, but it is variation on a theme.  Laws have always been written in response to a demand by those with influence among the lawmakers.  The number of people doing the demanding has fluctuated, and the channels through which they do the demanding has changed, but it's the same basic idea.

In a hierarchical institution, there are more subordinates than heads.  This means the subordinates can always remove the heads, but this requires cooperation.  It has to be game-theoretically feasible.  For it to be game-theoretically feasible, a potential reward from the head for thwarting a conspiracy has to be worth less than what would be gained by joining a conspiracy to remove it.  It's not quite so cut and dry in reality, of course, but that's where the equilibrium lies.

In tribes with low degrees of specialization and around 1 layer of hierarchy, this meant the leader must listen to almost everyone's concerns (as long as other people would listen to them).  In a society with more hierarchical layers like Egypt or Sumeria, there were likely a few important advisors that the king or emperor depended on.  Military, for one was certainly important.  In order to enact authority, the leader would have needed to at least not make those trusted in the court too angry.

Of course what would make them angry would depend on their position.  A military leader would tend to want to keep his army happy.  Landowners would want to keep the peasants alive to work at least, if not a little happy.

Let's assume that this society has 3 cities of 100 people each.  Each city has 3 landowners, each of which has 3 overseers, each of which has 3 slaves under his/her (most likely his, because this is such a patriarchal-society thing to do) "guidance."  Just to make the numbers work, we'll say they each have a wife and a kid, and there are a few left over who are merchants or something.

To stop themselves from being oppressed in a civilized manner, 1 slave would have to talk to the other slaves in his group and decide to make demands to the overseer.  The overseer, if he were nice, would empathize, but he would have to convince the other overseers to talk to the land owner.  Then the landowners would have to talk to the mayors, and the mayors would all have to talk to the king.

Not gonna happen.  What if all the slaves are so pissed that they all talk to their overseers?  What's the likelihood of that all happening at the same time?  What's the likelihood of them getting taken seriously?  The overseers have an intuitive understanding of this dynamic, and know that they don't really have to listen to the slaves.

Then, of course, there's always the revolt.  This isn't really what anybody wants, but you can only take so much.

As more layers are inserted, the distance between most people and the law-makers increases.  Every level of the hierarchy weights the game steeply in favor of every higher level.

Of course, there's an equilibrium that can be reached where the people at the higher levels are nice enough to prevent the lower levels from getting too angry.  But the tendency is for the hierarchy to increase.  Societies tend to grow, especially a hierarchical one like this setup, and humans tend to work themselves into hierarchies when they're in big groups.
