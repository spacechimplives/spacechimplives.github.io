A fundamental assumption of science up to this point is that the perceptible world is all that exists.  This has no evidence to back it up.  Since the dawn of science we have continually run into inexplicable phenomena, and developed models to account for them.  The way I see it is that we have a very coarse resolution of an infinite universe.  Of course I am not alone in this idea, but if you take it to its end point, it provides a new way of understanding that could potentially provide tools for refining the resolution of our lens.

One way to imagine this is that each quantum/particle/wave/string is a set of states in infinite dimensions (or degrees of freedom as I sometimes refer to them), with some correlations among all dimensions.  We are composed of matter in certain states, and hence we can only perceive matter that has a threshold amount of energy in those dimensions that we inhabit.

The limits of our perception--Planck's constant and the speed of light--have to do with the ability to pull matter into our dimensions.  It requires work (or initial conditions) to create an asymmetry in the distribution of states along those dimensions.  Let's presume that the natural equilibrium for all dimensions is 0.  To modify the state along 1 dimension would require at least a slight change in the whole universe, due to the correlations among all dimensions (which is infinite, but presumably some correlations are weaker than others to the point where the total work required would converge).

Like a generator where the current produces a magnetic field which resists its own motion, any change in state would have resistance.   Why?
