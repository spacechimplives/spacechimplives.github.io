I have a little commentary to make about [this story](http://abcnews.go.com/Politics/case-missed-obama-made-history-today-calls-female/story?id=27722408).  Quick summary: Obama only called on female reporters at a press conference.

Some people will say "this is discrimination, too."  And they're technically right by the definition of discrimination.  It is not treating everyone as if sex is not a thing.  It is making a distinction.

However when we were taught "discrimination is bad" in school, it isn't because the word discrimination is evil, it's because the actual effects of it are.  And this "reverse discrimination" does not have the same real-life consequences as the the original modern use of the word.

Every time someone whose voice was suppressed gets to speak up, we get more total information as a society than we had before.  The number of memes in our society increases at a faster rate than when someone whose voice is often heard speaks yet again.  Our memetic diversity increases.

But let's use a story to work it out a little better.

> So two people are sailing out from California trying to get to Australia.  One of them is a little bigger than the other: lets call him Bob, and the smaller one Phil.  They don't have a compass or anything, so they start arguing about which way is the right way.  In the mean time, as they're catching fish, Bob starts taking more than his share, and drinking more of the water.  Phil doesn't say anything immediately, because he doesn't notice at first, thinks "OK, he's a little bigger", and also doesn't want to start a conflict on a long voyage.  Meanwhile, Phil starts to be 100% sure they're headed in the wrong direction.  He tells Bob, "This is definitely the way to Alaska.  You are going exactly the wrong way."
> 
> "Nooo, this is definitely the right way."
> 
> "Bob, the sun rises in the East and sets in the West.  We are clearly going North"
> 
> "How would you know?  My gut's telling me this is the way, so this is where we're going."
> 
> Eventually, Bob is eating even more of the fish and drinking more of the water, and Phil is starting to get hungry.
> 
> "Please share your fish, Bob.  I'm starting to get really hungry here."
> 
> "No, see, if I stay strong, then I can catch more fish, so we can both have more fish!"
> 
> Phil reaches for some water, and Bob smacks it out of his hand and punches him.  Just then Phil points to a kayaker, and Bob can no longer deny that they were gong the wrong way.  So Phil grabs a piece of fish and turns the boat the right way.  After a while, goes to sleep.
> 
> While Phil is asleep, Bob gets a gut feeling and changes the boat's course.  Phil wakes up and says "What the hell, Bob?  Now we're headed to Japan"
> 
> Bob says, "No way.  I know where we're going.  And we're not headed North anymore."  Then, as he smacks some fish out of Phil's hand, "Hey, that's mine.  You didn't catch any fish, you just slept.  Plus you're so weak, you couldn't catch fish if you wanted to.  I'm doing all the work, so I get the fish."

See where I'm going with this?  Just because we're not headed in exactly the wrong direction, doesn't mean we're headed in the right direction.  Just because women are allowed to ask questions at press conferences now, doesn't mean they're allowed to ask the questions that they want.  Just because the patriarchy has ceded a little ground, like letting women vote, doesn't mean that women really have a voice.

We've gone so far in the wrong direction that it's not going to be some minor course adjustment to get back to where we want to go.  We're going to have to listen to other people who might have a better idea of where we should head and allow some intellectual diversity in the guidance of our system.

All of the information that these old rich white men use to determine the future of our country comes filtered.  It is abstracted information.  They may say "oh, look that the number of people below the poverty line.  That's such a shame, we should fix that."  And then, even with the best of intentions, their actions will be guided by trying to change the number.  Someone who has actually lived it will have tangible, real-world information about what needs to be fixed and how to do it.

But there's this narrative that the uneducated don't know what's best for them.  It's been around since the time of Marie Antoinette.  This idea can only be maintained by extreme isolation.  Information tends to diffuse out everywhere, but with a larger income gap, information has to jump further, and the rate of diffusion slows down, it gets easier to stay isolated.

That isolation goes both ways to some extent.  While the "Let them eat cake" types are isolated from all kinds of information that bleeds much more freely through the rest of society, the rest of society also tends to be isolated from the language that those people use.  People who treat human lives like pieces on a chessboard to not respond to emotional overture.  They respond to numbers and ROI.  These numbers can be essentially made up at will, but they don't understand that, and they won't process anything without it being broken down into little pieces they can understand like a parent cutting a piece of meat for their child.

One way to effect change is to publish papers using standard statistical methods that challenge the status quo.  With the advent of R and other languages like that, it can be done very easily.  In fact, it could be done much more cheaply by crowd-sourcing than through academic institutions, which have all kinds of overhead to deal with, like wine and cheese parties and greek life.
