So language basically hooks on to the interplay between perception and behavior.  It can modulate both.  For instance, if you're camping, you might call a log your "chair."  This doesn't change the properties of the object, but it changes the way you're primed to perceive it and the way you behave around it.

It calls attention to specific properties that make it chair-like.. a sufficiently flat horizontal surface on top, the ability to support a person's weight, a bottom-shape that won't move to much when you shift weight a little.  By modulating attention, language can modulate perception and behavior, thus having causal consequences in the world.

But even independently, any concept can do this.  When a monkey sees a sufficiently strong branch, it must have some way of identifying it as such.  It probably has experience with trees being rotted, and the branch then breaking.  This concept would then have an effect on the way the monkey behaved.  Perceiving its color, the way it bends, missing patches of bark, etc. it would hesitate to jump.  Only through experience (i.e. an interplay between perceiving and behaving) with branches with these kinds of properties can it form that concept.

This information is distilled from perception, but it has a distinctly functional flavor to it.  Without function, the concept would not exist.  The concept is essentially a pairing between the perceptual indicators, and the salient characteristics that define the function.  Upon getting sufficient evidence that a branch meets the definition of "rotted," there is a bifurcation in the possible actions the monkey will take.  "Jump onto that branch" would be inhibited, so this action would be much less likely to occur.

A complex integration of concepts like this, along with internal (scared, adrenaline rush--engage in riskier behavior) and external (It's raining, branches slippery--concentrate and grasp harder) cues will ultimately determine the outcome of the behavior.  Some of these pairings are still being done consciously, and some have been sufficiently learned, and are being done unconsciously.  Once it is integrated, a cascade of sub-routines fires off, and they are immediately modulated by the context.  The monkey's foot slipped a little, so it has to reach out further.  Oh, it overcorrected and hit the branch with its wrist, so it has to move its wrist in this certain way that it has learned by doing this countless times.  The branch is dead, so it can't take much stress, so just swing lightly and move to the next available branch.

The further up in the hierarchy of subroutines this is happening, the longer the time-scale on which these things are planned and re-planned.  Also, it is more likely to be a conscious behavior.  The more sub-behaviors that a super-behavior is made from, the longer it takes to perform, and therefore, less practice can be achieved.  Attention is a limited resource.  If a sub-goal has not been well-rehearsed, it requires attention, and thus a broader goal is unlikely to be achieved if it requires attention as well.

The more comfortably you sit within your repertoire of sub-routines, the more you can attend to longer-term, goal-directed behavior.  This is why expertise takes practice.  It is also why people get nervous.  Nervousness is attending to things sub-consciously.  Someone who thinks they forgot to turn off the oven is paying attention to their oven in the back of their mind, and weighing the cost of going back to turn it off with the (likelihood*cost=)risk of it being on.  A threshold likelihood of bad things will weigh on someone's mind.

The less you have to attend to the lower-level elements, the more you can attend to the higher-level elements.
