Without a society that could organize to build defensive structures and train a military, the Vikings would have taken over all of Europe.  But, with all the humans running around Europe that didn't want to deal with the Vikings, that organization was almost inevitable.  And, with their pressure, the Vikings created the environment for it to happen.  Since they would destroy any society that wouldn't organize to defend itself, the only society which would survive is the type that would defend itself.

Now, this is getting dangerously close to Social Darwinism, which is perhaps why the view of societies as organisms went by the wayside, but let me state how it is different.

First off, Social Darwinism was a statement about the genetic details of populations.  The ideas I intend to continue outlining are completely unrelated to the genetics of a society.  They are merely about the institutions and ideas that make up a society, and I think very little of psychology is genetically determined.  The overwhelming factors are social and developmental.  For all practical purposes, anything that comes from this theory can assume that genetic change happens on a slow enough time scale that you would totally ignore it in any analysis.

Second, there is a factor that Social Darwinism doesn't take into account--sexual selection.  In human societies, this is far and away the driving force of what little genetic drift there is.  In situations where animals have few if predators and ample food, they compete through aesthetics rather than exhausting battle.  Look at all the different kinds of animals in the rainforest and coral reefs.  Of course the whole point of sexual reproduction is increasing genetic diversity.  We now know how vital that is to the genetic health of a population.  Humans have no predators other than each other, and through most of recorded history, it seems that they have had plenty of food for the most part.  The wars that we have had don't seem to have had a major effect on natural selection either, broad groups of humans are all similar heights and weights, and it seems to be even more similar when corrected for nutrition.

Another fact which is fundamental to a lot of my ideas is that the diversity within any division is greater than between divisions at the same scale.  This has been proven over and over genetically, and I would like to prove it

But, one thing that we don't think enough about is intellectual diversity.  The amount of information that is implicit in a culture is vastly underestimated.  As much as I don't like Richard Dawkins' attitude, the idea of memes has irrefutable merit, and memetic diversity is under-appreciated and under-studied.
