So how does a continuous gradient along a dimension get treated as a boundary?  There is no truly empty space between the earth and the moon.  There are only continuous boundaries in space.. or if there are discrete boundaries, they only exist at the quantum level.

The fact that the moon and earth are distinct from space and each other has profound physical effects.  The earth is warmed by the sun's radiation.  The moon is as well, however, that warmth diffuses quickly on the dark side.  The space in between?  Yes, but not nearly at the scale.  The density of matter is so many orders of magnitude higher on the earth and moon that the sun's radiation treats them differently.  From the radiation's point of view, they are distinct.  "Of course the radiation doesn't know the difference between the earth and the vacuum of space, it's just acting according to lower-level physical laws."  Of course.  But, if you took a ride on one particular photon, its behavior would start reacting to the mass of earth before it even got there.  There are a few different ways of imagining why this would be the case.

First, you could say it's just obeying to the law of general relativity.  From this perspective you're basically saying that the mass of the earth deforms the space around it (presumably via emitted gravitons) and any light waves, or photons are just following the shortest-distance paths to their destinations.  It seems reasonable enough.  When water is flowing down, we know that it will flow between two hills, rather than over one or the other.  This is basically the same thing, but rather than minimizing height, it's time that is being minimized.
