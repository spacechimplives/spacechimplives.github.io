Let's take for granted the assumption that an institution's business processes are able to be modeled via a Chaitin numbering.  If 2 sets of business processes differ only by one change, there should be some minimal way to translate between their Chaitin numberings.  We can say then that these programs are "close" to each other.  Significantly different sets of business processes should then be considered "far" from each other, and require more or more complex transformations to become equivalent.

Transitioning to a new set of business processes itself also should require energy proportional to the distance of the new business processes. This is the way an organization manages to exhibit "behavior".  A business process takes energy

One could conjecture that a certain combination of information and energy are required to influence the "velocity" of an institution (if you model its processes as a space, the velocity is its rate of travel through that space). How many bits of information go from me into the government's decision-making process? Very few, right? That means there's a limited channel capacity from my intentions of the behavior of the federal government, and its actions. Let's sum the channel capacities of all people to the decisions of the federal government. And then lets compare that to the total amount of energy it's expending. If the bits of information are too low in comparison to the energy the government is expending using that information, that means the trajectory has no guarantee of being stable, predictable, and in the interests of the people.

All living things produce information.  Let's imagine a simple organism with 1 degree of freedom both for sensation and action.  If it senses a concentration of its randomly fluctuating energy source which is higher than its internal concentration, it opens a membrane, allowing energy to flow in at a rate proportional to the difference.  If it senses a lower concentration, then it closes the membrane.  Both the sensing of the concentration, as well as the action of its membrane consume energy.  Sensing in this case would be a form of sampling, so depending on the sampling rate, will require a different amount of energy, with varying levels of efficiency depending on that.
