So I think we can make the statement that what the experimenter has control over is encoded in the initial conditions they record, in the form of parameters to the equations… then we can say that the final state will be calculated according to the equations and those results will be compared to values that are measured.

I think it stands to reason that if there is no equation, the experimenter encodes no initial conditions, they compare no values to final conditions, so we can say that there is no useable work done within the thermodynamic work described.  So according to that thermodynamic system, none of the energy is "information" energy, and all of it is "entropy" energy.

We can also consider a system which doesn't use equations to encode the information about its experiments.  Presumably it would fall under the same constraints, whether that information were stored in neurons, DNA, protein configurations, etc.  

This means that for any individual to verifiably extract work from a system, they need to understand and predict its behavior.  If they can't verify whether work has been extracted, then it stands to reason they cannot use this possibly-extracted energy to then verifiably do any other work... otherwise they'd be verifying that work was done previously.

What I'm trying to get at---I want to say even independent of experiments and equations... as a person I can't extract work from a system whose behavior I can't predict. I can have that information stored in my brain/genes/ribosomes etc rather than an equation, either way it's "information" which allows me to extract work. Without that information, I cannot harness the work... without that information, from my perspective, it's entropy.

If we can go a step further and determine the amount of work required to create a certain amount of information about a system, we can also set lower bounds on the amount of energy required to make the system behave in a certain way with a certain probability.  

I suspect we should able to do this by using the relationship between momentum and position.  Positioning a particle in a region of a certain size with a certain probability should require equivalent energy to giving it a certain amount of momentum with a certain probability.  Translating that to kinetic energy, we should be able to determine a minimum amount of energy required to constrain a particle to a given region or locate the particle within a given region.   Knowing that multiple particles are in a given space with a given probability allows us to make predictions about what will happen next, such as probability of two atoms reacting chemically etc.
