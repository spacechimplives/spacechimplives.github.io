The interplay among language, behavior, and sensation is really complex, but there are some similarities.

First is that they all are made up of some units that can be substituted for each other to make up a new "recipe".

 	- In language, the word "clouds" is a plural noun.  In some places, you can substitute "birds" for "clouds" and everything else remains the same.  If you say "There are clouds in the sky." versus "There are birds in the sky."  The meanings are almost exactly the same.  The only difference would be incidental effects.  Like if you're hungry and have some way of getting the birds out of the sky, or if you're particularly concerned about the weather that day.

 	- In behavior, you can have "routines" that can be substituted for each other.  Curly braces are to distinguish the action itself from a linguistic command: {Go to the river} {catch a fish} {bring it back} {put it on the fire}.   Versus: {Go to the river} {fill a bucket with water} {bring it back} {put it on the fire}.

 	- In perception, we can separate out properties of an object from the object.  So we can have [big][blue][cup][with handle] or [small][blue][cup][with handle], as well as all kinds of variations with different concepts substituted.

They recombine in hierarchical fashion.

 	- Words come together to form sentences.

 	- {Go get water} is a compression of multiple steps that each need to be carried out.

 	- The cup is perceived as a whole object in spite of its separate properties

Those hierarchical structures have complex dependencies

 	- You have to use "are" rather than "is" before "clouds."  In other words, the form is dependent on the contents.  You would tend to say "There are clouds in the sky" in a formal setting, but might also say "There's clouds in the sky" in an informal one.  The form is also dependent on the context.

 	- You have to have a bucket in mind and get it before you can fill it with water.   You have to get a fishing pole before you can fish.  Sub-steps like these are dependent on the intention of the behavior as well as the context (is there a bucket I always leave by the river?  Then I don't need to fetch it first).

 	- A cup that is bigger than most that someone has experienced will still be perceived as the small one if it is surrounded by larger cups (dependent on context).  A cup made of ceramic that has a handle might be called a mug, but if it's a small disposable one made of paper, it probably won't (dependent on contents/structure)
