The [free energy principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_energy_principle) is a proposed theory about systems which make predictions about the world reducing the uncertainty in the form of something called "variational free energy".  Variational free energy is essentially the difference between how a system expects the world to behave and how it does behave.

![](http://spacechimplife.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/500px-MarokovBlanketFreeEnergyFigure-300x225.jpg) ![](http://spacechimplife.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/220px-Uexkulls_schema_original.jpg)

Interestingly, It seems to be a pretty direct mathematical formalization of the concept of the umwelt from "theoretical biology" by von Uexküll.

It also provides a compelling mathematical model for considering the concept of representation within a system with emergent dynamic properties.  It seems to validate some of the ideas in "representation and behavior" by fred keijzer, for instance the concept of internal control parameters (ICP's) which are self-organized.  The system is able to adjust its behavior through "modulating" these ICP's.  As well as the idea that "all behavior is anticipatory" (p 234).

In the model put forth by the free energy principle, the ICP's are the internal states, which, through sensation and action become coupled to hidden states in the environment.

**Minimizing surprise**

People commonly refer to the clarifying redefinition of shannon entropy as "average surprise".  So, if you model the processes between the internal states and external states as a communication, then in order to "minimize surprise" you would need to minimize the shannon entropy of messages coming from  the environment.  At first this might seem impossible.  If the message is coming from the environment, and the shannon entropy is inherent to the message, how can an organism change the shannon entropy.

This requires adding another element to the model which is that the messages from the environment are actually created by the organism... by its receptors.  Thus it must organize its receptors to create this communication channel which transforms continuous environmental signals into the "alphabet" of the message.

By modulating the characteristics of that alphabet or code in response to the messages received, the average surprise can be minimized.  For instance, this could be done by modifying the code used to make common messages shorter
