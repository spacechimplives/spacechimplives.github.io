"Nevertheless, it is necessary to remember that a planned economy is not yet socialism. A planned economy as such may be accompanied by the complete enslavement of the individual"... "How can the rights of the individual be protected and therewith a democratic counterweight to the power of bureaucracy be assured?"
--Albert Einstein, [*Why Socialism*](https://monthlyreview.org/2009/05/01/why-socialism/)

Einstein has a nice little essay where he gives insight into his sociological beliefs. Sometimes it's hard to believe how eventful his life was, achieving such great success, being in contact with all the other greatest minds of his time, yet having started off as a Jew in Nazi Germany, and leaving as it became illegal for him to continue his work.

Anyway, he viewed society as something kind of like a bee or ant colony with a major difference that humans have memory and language to an extent that we can spend time on things that are not strictly for survival.  The key thing that he saw as similar was interdependence.  He stated "It is only a slight exaggeration to say that mankind constitutes even now a planetary community of production and consumption."

But he also emphasized, with the quote on top, the danger that a planned economy without the right checks and balances could be something other than socialism that strips away people's freedoms.

However, the quote I find most interesting is here:
"The essential point about this process is the relation between what the worker produces and what he is paid, both measured in terms of real value. Insofar as the labor contract is “free,” what the worker receives is determined not by the real value of the goods he produces, but by his minimum needs and by the capitalists’ requirements for labor power in relation to the number of workers competing for jobs. It is important to understand that even in theory the payment of the worker is not determined by the value of his product."
