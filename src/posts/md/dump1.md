However we can follow this thread further in several ways and start to find ideas that do push the boundaries.

For instance, we can see where organisms are for the most part.  They tend to have a clear boundary in space from our perspective.  But organizations are not.  How can we define the boundary of an organization?  If organizations are akin to life, how does their metabolism work?  They must consume energy and distribute it to its members like other life. What are the sensory receptors of a human institution and how do they collect and transmit information.  Where to they transfer it to?  If business logic organizes humans to do specialized work for an organization, and the genetic code organizes cells to do specialized work for an organism... is there a level below that, or above that?

Perhaps the biggest question down this line is: what is this information?  How does energy get turned into it?  How does it then allow us to extract more energy from our environment more effectively?  How much more effective does it make us?

If the system is not directly providing that energy, it must be doing so indirectly.  People would quickly go extinct if they worked at the airport instead of gathering food unless they are somehow provided the energy they are skipping out on collecting while working there.  We can therefore use a rough form of the conservation of energy to ballpark the amount of human energy that goes into the airport to make it run in terms of the power required to sustain a human as well as the number of full time equivalent employees.  This turns out to be about 400 watts * about 50000 employees = ~20 MW.

If they are being provided energy in exchange for working at the airport by some system, then that system must be getting energy from somewhere.  If not, it would cease to be able to provide energy to its constituents including the airport, which would in turn cease to be able to provide energy to those working there, who would then cease to be able to provide energy to their heart and brain cells.  We know from experience that this is done with money.  We also know from experience that airports do not produce energy.  So we can assume that the energy required to sustain the airport is coming from somewhere else.  It uses the fundamental assumption that employees can rely on their money to provide them energy.  Its business logic is coupled to the business logic of another system.  It has an external dependency on a system which provides an interface that the airport consumes.
