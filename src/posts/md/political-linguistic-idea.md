Apparently,  [this is where we are in terms of political dialogue](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RJeugIJFXas). Society is splitting into two more and more separate groups.

What this video tells me is that these two groups literally cannot communicate because they do not share any of the same concepts, or at least that the channel they communicate over is noisier and noisier.

While they use the same "closed-class" words (e.g. if, up, down, when, is, can---the most fundamental words we use), less and less of their "open-class" vocabulary (e.g. astronaut, artichoke, slither, monstrous, concept---basically words that have synonyms) has the same connotations.  This is a common problem that can slow everyone down, but if it becomes such an issue that daily tasks start requiring significantly more work, it can become more economically efficient for the two groups to stop working with each other.

First, let's define what I mean when I say the groups cannot communicate.  The two sides are quickly developing their own jargons based on what they hear and read.  In my experience, reaching common ground in a political conversation these days requires analyzing the most basic ideas.  So basic that it gets into philosophy.  For instance what are sex and gender?  Are taxes OK?  Is war good or bad?  Should a person be allowed to be a citizen if they are muslim? Is it OK for the government to feed people who don't have jobs?  20 years ago, none of these were questions that anyone had to ask.

When you're having a conversation about whether to fund healthcare, it is difficult enough when you have the same ideas of what a life is, or what health consists of.  In order for intellectual work to be done, the fundamental concepts must be agreed upon before a corollary can be developed.

It is reductionist to consider just "the right" and "the left" but it is becoming more and more a reality as our institutions diverge.  Before there was "academia."  Now there are totally separate universities and journals publishing fundamentally opposed research on very basic ideas.  Is the earth warming?  It is deceptively difficult to answer that question because the data is gathered and analyzed in so many different ways.  Just a few years ago, there was much more fundamental agreement on basic ideas at the level of the policy-maker.  It has become clear that each side is working with a different set of facts.

I remember in the lead-up to the passing of Obamacare, there were people railing against "death panels."  I didn't know what they were talking about.  This was a concept that they had that I didn't.   I remember reading something about a group of doctors deciding what medications would be allowed under the plan. That concept was ridiculed by the left, but I doubt most people on the left quite understood why people on the right were worried about that.  I still don't.  If there was a statute involved in the plan, I never quite understood it.

Nowadays, you hear a lot of ridicule of the "anti-vaxxers."  However, I don't believe most people who ridicule them really know what their claim is.  For the most part as far as I can tell, they are not opposed to all vaccines in a blanket way.  Similarly, people on the right don't seem to understand at all what people mean when they say they identify as a different gender.  The concept is just not shared at all.

In real life when people misunderstand each other on a concept, they just kind of go down a meta level and get each other on the same page before moving back up to the level they were talking about.  This is how jargon usually works.  So when you talk to someone about opening their browser to check their IP address, then they might say "I don't know what a browser is" then you say "it's internet explorer or chrome or whatever you use to surf the internet." They say "OK" and then you both move on.

However if you were talking to someone who really knew nothing about computers, and they didn't know the words for "surf the internet" and they didn't know what internet explorer or chrome was, then obviously you're going to have a hard time getting on the same page.

So the two sides each have their own concepts.  I used to understand rightspeech, but I really don't anymore.  When I talk to someone and they start talking about how muslims want to destroy our freedom, I don't know where we can start to get on the same page.

I believe this is due to economic dissociation.  The winning strategy in the country is totally different from the winning strategy in the city.  That isn't the only divide but it's a major one.  I should write another post to explain why, but I believe that in the city, specialization leads to maximizing revenue as a primary strategy, whereas in the country, minimizing costs by doing things oneself and maintaining independence are the best economic strategies.  This leads to fundamentally different goals in many areas of life.

When two languages collide, they often form a combination of the two languages called a "pidgin", and as it gets developed, a "creole".  One thing that has been observed is that the colonizing language tends to lend its open-class words, whereas the colonized lends its closed-class words.

This makes sense in a way, because the colonized people are forced to re-align their society to the concepts of the colonizing society.  In order to put food on their table, individuals have to do things the way of the colonizers, which requires understanding their concepts.  They are essentially aligning themselves to the goals of the colonizers because of the economic association.  This is not the only reason why open-class words might be the first to be adopted by the colonized, but it lends itself to an idea that relates to the rest of this article.

I have always assumed that when a language diverges into dialects it is due to geography and phonetic drift which leads to unintelligibility that then leads to economic dissociation.  But perhaps it could go the other way.

Perhaps the economic dissociation, or lack of shared goals, leads to an atrophy of shared concepts.  That then leads to lack of daily interaction as it is less productive, which leads to a lack of shared environment, and potentially relocation towards those with shared goals.

It's possible that in the past, linguistic drift happened more due to geography, but now with the internet, geography is not the primary boundary between communicating groups.  Perhaps the bottleneck is a more abstract one like class or economic strategy.
