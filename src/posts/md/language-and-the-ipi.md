When you apply any language, you are dividing the world into subsets.  These subsets are not strictly defined.  In fact, they are not strictly definable unless you have already limited the set to something that is undefinable.

So, when a person sets about to define "Canadian," they usually would say something like "People from Canada."  This clearly is not a sufficient distinction.  There are plenty of people who have left Canada who are not Canadian.  You might say "No, I just mean people who were born in Canada."  This is also not sufficient.  There are almost certainly people who were born in Canada who neither identify themselves as Canadian, nor are citizens of Canada.  Then you might say "OK, then citizens of Canada are Canadian."  So then you're limiting yourself to the government's definition.  Well, do you think there might be some people who are not documented within Canada's citizen database who are Canadian?  Almost certainly.  What about a child who is just born?  What about a fetus?  Then you have to define what a person is, and on and on.

To take a dose of my own medicine, I will attempt to define "define."  The way I mean to use it is to select from a group every real object that is part of the category, and reject every object that is not part of the category.

If you try to do this, you will find that every time you try to do it, you will end up having to delineate what it is to exist and other unanswerable questions.  The fact is that language is something we apply to the world.  Even when we delineate categories that line up very conveniently with objective reality, like "my car," there are extreme situations where our definitions would break down.

However, despite the fact that linguistic categories are an illusion, they're one that we share, and that has very practical, objective, physical effects in the universe.

The most obvious from of behavioral constraint that an institution can wage is through laws.  These are carefully selected words, some of which have very carefully delineated meanings specific to the implementation of the law.  And they can cause a chain of reactions which result in humans doing things.

Laws epitomize the complications of translating language and ideas into real-world behaviors.  The judicial system attempts to leave room for interpretation through judge and jury, but the language of the law clearly does not serve its purpose sometimes.  The judge and jury are bound to interpret the language of the law as it was written, yet the language is imprecise, creates loopholes and unfortunate convictions.

The law is essentially the code of whatever body has one.  In student groups, we are encouraged to define guidelines for electing officers and outlining basic rules.  This can be convenient, because it allows the group to function without being paralyzed at every conflict, or having to reinvent the wheel, but there is an extreme dearth of diversity in the implementations of these schemes.

The example of student groups is one of the many, many situations where a group is forced to define certain elements to be a part of a higher organization.  These elements are the points of interaction between institutions.  Standardizing those points of interaction is something like an API that is required to interact within a society.  An API is code internal to an application that the author writes so that another program can pass data back and forth or use the functionality of the program.

So what I mean is there is a specific language that has to be configured between 2 institutions for them to interact with each other.  This is true between individuals and institutions as well.

When you deal with legal issues, you are generally required to identify yourself.  This can be with a driver's license or social security number sometimes, but there is usually a standardized way of doing this.  When you talk to your family, you don't need to do anything special to identify yourself.  This is not something inherent to human life.

There are other kinds of institutional interactions that have different institutional communication frameworks (ICFs).  When you buy a coffee, it can be anonymous.  The only information transferred between you and the coffee chain is a number indicating the price of your order.  Though, this is changing rapidly--retail outlets are [beginning to use facial recognition technology](http://www.nytimes.com/2014/02/02/technology/when-no-one-is-just-a-face-in-the-crowd.html) to identify people in the store for marketing purposes.

In interviewing for a job, there is all kinds of information transferred, from the clothes you're wearing (hopefully they won't be judgemental), to the tone of your voice, to the formatting of your resume, and of course, your experience and demeanor.  But that information gets routed to different parts of the institution once it is inside.  There is probably some sort of interview sheet with questions and checklists that they store in a certain office.  All of the information about your appearance isn't necessarily transferred to anyone who didn't see you.  Also, they start to forget you as soon as you walk out the door.  You get distilled into less information, but they captured general information about you on the checklist.

The point is that the ICF can be modeled as a communication channel with limited bandwidth.  The transactions can take the form of physical tokens, spoken words, currency, video, or an imprint on someone's mind, but it is all information being transferred to the institution.  It is finite and has a certain amount of entropy.

The institution also communicates back.  In the instance of buying coffee, this is actually the primary direction of the communication.  They have architects and interior designers design their stores, train their cashiers, put their prices on the menu, and advertise with a sign.  In the case of an interview, they will tell you if you were rejected or not, ask you if you have questions, and give you a rundown of benefits of being employed with them.  The rest is generally not as consciously designed as a retail experience, depending on the company and position.

The thing is, for it to be an institutional communication or behavior, it has to be chosen through their institutional processes.  Their laws or policies.   A Starbucks hires an architect on contract (this is spectulation) specifically to design stores, using all the institutional processes required to hire a contractor there.  They have to file paperwork about that transaction that interfaces with the IRS and when they build the store, they have to clear it with zoning and environmental regulatory institutions.  But at each step in the path, more information is generally lost.

The point is, very much of this information has a finite, traceable path, and finite content.  The process of information moving through an institution *is* abstraction.  It is the process of physical reality being stripped of more and more characteristics until it can be processed.  Then the institution processes that abstracted information, and translates it into a behavior.  At each step of the behavior, it changes from an abstraction to something closer to physical reality until it is an actual building or communication.

This is the process of organic behavior.  Studying it can open a window into our own minds, but it can also allow us to reign in our institutions before they spiral out of control and force us, each individual, into collective actions that we would not otherwise be doing.
