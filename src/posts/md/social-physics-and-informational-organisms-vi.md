So what are some elements of a unified model of social physics and how might they be tested?

First, the testing could be done with big data from social media.   But another treasure trove of this kind of info is cryptocurrencies, which store all kinds of information in a way that can easily be tied to time, individual, etc. as well as offering different governance models to look at.  It is the perfect domain for testing out various social and economic models.

As for the elements.  I think there's a great reason to think that lots of existing elements of physics will hold.  The hard matter is translating those elements into something measurable.

So for instance the relationship of entropy, energy and temperature as

\[S=\int{\frac{dQ}{\tau}}\]

or

\[ \Delta S = \int{\frac{\delta Q_{reversible}}{T}} \]

is probably still going to hold because it is based on the fundamental laws of thermodynamics.  The work will be in extending these concepts in a coherent way to information as it is used at the human scale.

This relationship really catches my eye, because it kind of says a lot that comes out of nowhere.  \(TS\) comes out to be equivalent to energy.  There doesn't seem to be any obvious reason why that makes sense or would be the case by our everyday definitions of temperature and entropy.  And yet is is fundamental to all physics.  So we have to break these definitions down a little further.

So a good example to illustrate this is a pot boiling.  The boiling is not reversible, so the second equation doesn't apply.  So as you're increasing the temperature of the pot, you're increasing \(\tau\).  This requires you to burn gas or electricity.  The total kilowatt hours put into boiling the pot is \(dQ\).   So essentially this equation is showing that if you're putting energy into this pot but it is not increasing the temperature, then it is increasing the entropy... increasing the probability of occupying more states, or reducing the constraint of degrees of freedom.  So basically the particle must be increasing the number of states in which it can possibly be.  For those water molecules, this could mean expanding the spatial region where they can be, by evaporating, or increasing the momentum region they can be by moving around with greater velocities.  It doesn't matter, essentially it is "filling" its degrees of freedom and "finding" more.

What does it mean to "find" another degree of freedom?  A water molecule exhibits 6 degrees of freedom of motion as well as excitation states, etc.  Well, if you keep raising the temperature, the electrons are more probably being excited in to higher energy levels.  At some point it goes beyond being a gas to being a plasma, where suddenly the electrons have more than orbital degrees of freedom, they also have spatial degrees of freedom.  That increases the entropy.

Temperature we just think of as hot and cold, but at the level of small particles it is the average kinetic energy.

For entropy, at the most microscopic levels, we get the equation:

\[S = -k_{b}\sum{ p_{i} ln p_{i} }\]

And for temperature we get the equation

\[T =\frac{1}{2} \sum{ m_{p} v_{p}^2 } \]

expanding that to put the velocity in to the same coordinates we use for entropy we get:

\[T =\frac{1}{2} \sum{ m_{p} \sqrt{ \sum{v_{pi}^2} } } \]

Now this assumes euclidean space, so these definitions are inherently classical.  These definitions are probably readily transferrable into relativistic coordinates, however, by modifying the metric term.

Now when we look at the term \(ST\) we get some messy double sum.. but at least we're dealing in terms that are more readily applicable to the kinds of examples we will be using, like energies of individual particles.
