So in order to develop a human program, first we have to understand what exactly we would expect it to do.  In other words, write some pseudo-tests.

So one question is whether we are dealing with individual humans or larger groups.  They both have their pros and cons.  Ultimately, both are probably done in nature.

A pro of dealing with individual humans is that it would be much easier to devise experiments.  However, individual humans are unpredictable and if you try to implement a program that requires high fidelity transmission of parameters through multiple functions--as is the case with all but the most rudimentary EE side of classical computing--it will probably not be possible.

Ultimately, they are just different scales of the same basic things.  Group "programming" is to neural nets as individual "programming" is to individual transistors.
