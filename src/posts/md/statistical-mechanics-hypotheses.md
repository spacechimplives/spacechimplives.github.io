There are assumptions I make when I’m writing about statistical mechanics-adjacent topics. I say adjacent because some of the assumptions I make may violate the postulates of statistical mechanics.

Here I will illustrate the assumptions and related hypotheses and make an attempt to justify them or come up with a way to test them

(1) Unless otherwise constrained, an object can be assumed to have a uniform probability of occupying any available states. A continuously adjacent set of states, I refer to as a “degree of freedom”

(2) Unless otherwise constrained, objects tend to meander through available states according to random walks, with no given dependence on historical states.

(3) Removing a constraint will result in a higher-entropy probability distribution

(n) A constraint is the introduction of an asymmetry to the probability of a transition between two states. Maintaining such an asymmetry requires energy. Without maintenance, the asymmetry will shrink and disappear. While maintained, such asymmetry causes unevenness in the probability distribution, or “negentropy” or “information”.

(n) Objects will tend to move from states which give them lower-entropy probability distributions to states which give them higher-entropy probability distributions at a rate proportional to (a) the difference of entropy (b) the temperature (global rate at which states change) (c) the inverse of the internal energy of the object (inertia… i.e. how many coupled objects also need to change state)
