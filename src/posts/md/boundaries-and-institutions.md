[Here](http://www.ribbonfarm.com/2015/03/04/gardens-need-walls-on-boundaries-ritual-and-beauty/) is a great piece which breaks down how essential semi-permeable membranes are for complexity to develop.

This is an essential part of my ideas about institutions.  How does an institution maintain boundaries?

First, there is the aesthetic selection process

[![cog5-lecppt-chapter08-7-638](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/cog5-lecppt-chapter08-7-638.jpg?w=300)](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/cog5-lecppt-chapter08-7-638.jpg)In a context where an institution is competing with others serving the same purpose, an institution promotes itself through aesthetics.  Thus those who tend to engage with it will be those attracted to that aesthetic.  This is obvious in the marketing of a product, as well as religious art.  You can also find it organically develop in subcultures.  "Hipsterism" was originally associated with a certain style, and this fragmented into various sub-groups.  Grunge in the 90's had its music, clothes, and art.  But there is more to being a hipster than style.  Not much more, but something.  You aren't going to find many (if any) hipsters who drive a Hummer, or who supported the war in Iraq.  In cognitive science, there is the concept of "family resemblance" where a category is not defined by any one feature, but rather a shared set of features.  [![19-1](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/19-1.gif?w=300)](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/19-1.gif)It is associated with [prototype theory](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype_theory).  Chairs don't always have 4 legs.  They don't always have a back.  They aren't always used for sitting.  But the more of these characteristics that something has, the more likely you are to call it a chair.  If you try to draw up a definition that includes everything that is a chair and exclude everything that is not a chair, it will be difficult because you will keep running into exceptions.

Each new level of the prototype then has its own prototypical features.  It inherits features of its parent category, but has its own set of features on top of that, and a family resemblance structure can be used to model how well something fits into the category, too.

Another aspect of a category is that the more prototypical features they exhibit, the more likely they are to exhibit other prototypical features.  You could consider belonging to a category to be a statistical distance from some imaginary prototype.  If you assume that each of these features can either be on or off with the same probability for each characteristic, you can view the degree of [![640px-Binomial_distribution_cdf.svg](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/640px-binomial_distribution_cdf-svg.png?w=300)](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/640px-binomial_distribution_cdf-svg.png)belonging in a category as a position along the [cumulative binomial probability distribution function](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_distribution), where the "number of trials" is interpreted instead as the number of characteristics taken into account for belongingness in that category, and the cumulative probability is instead considered as a degree of fitness within a category, or a "probability of being considered part of X category."

[caption id="attachment_589" align="alignright" width="300"][![F1FjJ](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/f1fjj.jpg?w=300)](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/f1fjj.jpg) A car... kind of, sometimes[/caption]

The obvious problems with such a model are that the probability of each characteristic varies, but also that each characteristic is a prototypical category in itself that varies with the context.  So "has a waxed moustache" come in degrees.  You could have a slight curl on either end, or you could go full Dali.  Furthermore, some of the details of that particular property are modulated by the context.  So when you're looking for a prototypical hipster waxed moustache, there are subtle differences from the prototypical waxed moustache of the 1800's.  When you're thinking of the category "fruit" for adding to sangria, the protoype is slightly different from the "fruit" category for breakfast (except for those raging alcoholics who drink sangria for breakfast).  It doesn't seem like a big deal when viewing each property individually, but in sum, it does.  If you go to rent a car and they give you the thing in the photo to the right, you'll be surprised.  It has the prototypical features of a car: wheels, an engine, a steering wheel (i think)... but those are bicycle wheels.  They fit well into the category of "wheel" but they do not fit well into the category of "wheel for a car."  The engine doesn't fit well either.  So if the whole is cobbled together from atypical parts, it will not by prototypical itself.

So a more appropriate model would be not a simple binomial distribution, but one where each "trial" has a different "probability" and "trial's" "probability" is determined by the "cumulative probability" of a set of child "trials".  Translating that back into our prototype fitness model: an individual specimen's distance from the prototype should be modeled as the degree of possession of certain prototypical characteristics, within the context of the initial prototype.  So a car is a more car-y car if it has car-y wheels with car-wheel-y tires and those are made from a car-wheel-tire-y material and appropriate texture.

As you continue breaking down each characteristic, you see that at the base, there are no on/off characteristics.  Each characteristic is made up of some fluid category.  Let's take the size of the wheel.   Depending on the car, different wheel sizes are appropriate.  As you narrow down the car to a specific model, the wheels still have slightly different sizes.  These vary around a mean, probably in something resembling a normal distribution.   This size is something like a tolerance when it is a sub-characteristic of a prototype.  "Having a moustache" has tolerance parameters as well.  [![big-world3](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/big-world3.jpg?w=300)](http://spacechimplifecom.local/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/big-world3.jpg)The difference between stubble and a full moustache is fuzzy (pun intended post-hoc).  There is a length after which a moustache is no longer adds weight to the characteristic of "being a hipster", but is a whole other ball game (see left).  It certainly adds weight to the characteristic of "having a moustache."

Back on the topic of aesthetic filters: by exhibiting a given aesthetic, a group of people allow themselves to be distinguished.  Generally, by exhibiting that aesthetic, there is some self-imposed limitation.  When you have a beard as a male, it is harder to get a certain professional jobs.  So by aesthetically identifying as a hipster, you are limiting yourself to certain ways of being.

If you dress in a suit, you also limit yourself in some ways.  Most people will treat you with a professional distance.   You are sending a signal: "I care more about making money than deeper human interaction."  The more closely you follow certain manners (keeping your bottom button un-buttoned, having the right cut or brand of suit, etc.), the more closely you are identified as a member of that particular group, whether it has a name or not.

Furthermore, people will anticipate unseen characteristics based on those seen.  This is the process of stereotyping.  If your fitness for a certain category is x so far, people will use that to pull certain other characteristics from that prototype and anticipate them with a certain confidence.  "So far he seems to be about a .6 on the hipster scale, so I'm going to guess he has a tattoo."

So categories' boundaries are not discreet.  But then how is the boundary formed at all?  In Sara Perry's article, linked at the top, she mentions that the moon's boundary with earth is not permeable enough for earth's life to make the leap.  It is just not close enough to earth.  So this boundary is merely due to distance in this case.  The space between is filled with some things, but the part that makes it a barrier is nothing.  The void.

This is true of all boundaries.  In our model, there is a continuous distance from a prototypical configuration of elements.  As individuals converge towards the primary prototype, they converge in form, and can thus share more, and given enough individuals, these efficiencies are inevitably realized.  Most cars use the same few lugnut sizes.  There are semi-standard spark-plug sizes.  As there are more individuals revolving around the same prototype, you can almost model it as a kind of gravity, where future individuals will tend to be statistically closer to the prototype.  The prototype itself is a moving target as well.  Since the car is a tool of society, as the needs of society change, the prototypical form of a car will change to fit.

Between a car and a truck, there is a bit of empty space. It is partially filled by vans, SUV's, and the El Camino, but the density of the space in between is a bit lower than trucks or cars (historically, at least).  Between cars and trains, there is even more space.  There are [these](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Road-rail_vehicle), some of which are more like trains, and some of which are more like cars.  But in terms of sheer numbers, there are fewer.  The space between is continuous.  Something will tend to even out the space between.  However, that doesn't mean that that space will have no effects.  While it exists, it will maintain some asymmetry.  The Tao Te Ching says (..is translated to say)

> "The nameless is the origin of Heaven and Earth, The named is the mother of myriad things."

I interpret this to mean that "categorization" is what causes the diversity of the universe.  Of course, this is getting into my personal ideas about the nature of the universe, but it seems to jibe with what we know about physics at this point.  We start with just "it,"  "the stuff" Energy/Matter/Spacetime.  However any asymmetry in this has physical consequences.  When that asymmetry is then treated as constant by something moving on a quicker time scale, it becomes "named."  The sun will go out, yet the plants on the earth treat it as an inevitability.   Their genetic code has "named" the sun.  The orbit of the planets and asteroid around the sun is similar.  Inherent in their structure is the fact that the sun is dying much slower than they are moving around it.

Any asymmetry is a gradient across which work is done.  As entropy causes this asymmetry to even itself out, things move, and other things can piggy-back on them, creating more asymmetries within that main asymmetry.  As those start to even themselves out, yet more are created.  The scale of the asymmetry only matters in relative terms.   If the asymmetry is two buckets sitting next to each other--one with water, one without in a closed room, eventually they will reach the same level.  But in the meantime, every time a water molecule gets knocked off the surface of the water and becomes part of the ambient humidity, there is physical work being done on that water molecule.  The work required to get from the one state to the other is the distance from equilibrium.  The time that it takes to get to equilibrium is proportional to the number of paths that can be taken to get there.

So, the empty space between a bearded, tattooed hipster with a waxed moustache and a guy with a suit is real.  There are actual psycho-physical forces that filter out the space between to make it less likely for these two to interact and maintain that asymmetry.  Social asymmetries are caused by and result in these groups being informationally and psychologically isolated from each other.  Sure, there are some who work in finance for banks who have beards and tattoos, and grow vegetables in a window garden, but there is just less density of individuals with that sort of combo than there is within the clusters around the prototypes.  Eventually, they will even each other out, as more paths along that way are carved out.  Essentially instantly, water molecules start popping up between the two buckets, even if there were none before they were placed, and as each water molecule leaves the surface of the water of the full bucket, it slightly tugs on the molecules behind it.  Furthermore, it might smack back into the surface, disturbing more molecules.  In the meantime, it also decreases the temperature of the water.. a competing force.  That competing force starts out smaller, and slowly grows until the buckets reach equilibrium, and the combination of all competing forces nets 0.  In any asymmetry, such forces will exists, even if they exist below the quantum foam.  If the buckets are totally isolated on opposite ends of the universe, there will eventually be some set of events that evens out the amounts in them.  The time that it takes being proportional to the number of paths between the buckets.  Of course, the buckets may be destroyed before any of these paths are realized.

Anyway, moving away from conjectures about physics: After the aesthetic boundary comes the selection boundary.  I think the shared aesthetic among different subcultures is similar to lugnuts.  Differences catch people's attention.  However, attention requires energy.  If you have a beard at your financial office, people will notice it and make comments, even if only to say "hey, nice beard".  This will cause you to think about it.  People generally attempt to minimize the number of things they have to pay attention to on a daily basis.   When working on cars, it's great to not have to worry about the size of the lugnut.  As long as you have a standard crosswrench, you generally won't have to think about it.   If you have a car with 1", heptagonal lugnuts, you're going to need a special tool.  You'll have to make this new tool if one doesn't exist.  Next time you buy a car, you'll probably try to buy something with more normal lugnuts if you ever change your tires.  Of course these are not unbreakable rules.  But they are statistical tendencies, and that's what the boundaries are all about.

Not only is there some likelihood that you will shave your beard, but also there is a likelihood that they would never have hired someone with a beard in the first place.   Of course the beard alone wouldn't make a difference in your ability to analyze finances, but it may indicate you have the anti-corporate psychology that is often associated with hipsters.  Plus, the old guy that runs the bank might have given the hiring manager crap about his beard, and since he can't have one, he might subconsciously tend not to hire people with them.  That old guy running the bank might have his own reasons for not having a beard, and this filters down.  What Sarah Perry calls "tiling structures" are the result of these selection processes.  They are less about what is created, and more about what is discouraged.  The water is not disallowed from flowing from one bucket to the other.  It's just highly discouraged by the shape of the container.

It has to do with niche occupation.  Since things tend to fall apart, they require some protection to stay together.  If the earth was constantly getting smacked by comets, it would not exist as a unified earth for very long.  The Oort cloud and asteroid belt help us out on that front.  So does the atmosphere.  Through the boundary of our solar system, our Earth is allowed to exist long enough for some matter to be transferred back and forth between the moon.  Such transfer should only be expected to increase as debris from destroyed satellites orbiting earth gets shot out of orbit away from Earth and as more space programs send rovers or colonies there.  Whether these paths are purely physical, or become imaginary/informational paths for some parts of their trajectory is immaterial to the fact that they will form.

So only people attracted to the aesthetic will move toward a social space, and only those welcome in the social space will be allowed.  Further, as I said before, this prototype is a moving target.  The prototypical hipster looks different from 5 years ago.   Those who are closest aligned with the prototype will then affect the direction of this movement.  As they are pulled, so they pull.  Our bearded finance guy may, despite his beard, exhibit all the important properties of a finance guy and thus fit the prototype closely in spite of the one glaring difference.  This then reforms the mold around his beard.  Making that aspect of the mold less weighty.  The average beard of a finance person, weighted by how well they fit the finance person category, will be a little larger than it otherwise would be.  Eventually, as more do this, it will become a totally insignificant factor, or may even reverse.  As more fill the intermediate spaces between hipster and finance guy, the statistical distance between the two will close and the boundary will disappear.  The word "hipster" may fall out of use long before that.

So a boundary exists when, as you move away from a prototype, there is a sudden drop-off of density of individuals.  To some extent, this is because of forces pulling people and objects toward these prototypical spaces, but also because the lack of density in the spaces in between is a reflection of their inhospitability.  The density close to the prototype spaces is required for networking and sharing resources and information, which allows for more efficient allocation of resources, but with this comes constraints.  Wheels that are built out of tolerance will not become a part of a car, but will be broken down again and reformed, or used in a different kind of car with a wider tolerance.  Larger structures that exist within a denser system due to these networking effects break down faster as they navigate the space between.

The information that is transferred within the prototypical space also radiates out.  Hipsters have complex conversations about their craft beers.  Bits of this leak out into greater society and are used to either highlight differences or similarities.  Those who like craft beer will tend to talk to more hipsters as a result, and may get pulled into the orbit/whirlpool, following their own variation on the trajectory.  As they talk more, they will find it easier to use craft-beer terminology as the neural pathways surrounding those concepts are made more efficient, and it will create a positive feedback loop.  Those who don't like craft beer might avoid people who talk about it, creating a repulsion.
