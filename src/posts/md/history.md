Every major movement in history has an underlying philosophy behind it.  Of course I think the philosophy and the movement both come from the same cause, not that philosophy is somehow driving these movements.  Most major breakthroughs up until the early 1900s or so were still in some way tied into a basic philosophy made most popular with darwinism, but which predates him.  The basic elements of this philosophy are:

-- a source generating variations

-- a force selecting the fittest of those

-- which then go on to propagate

This basic model has been applied to everything from economics to neuroscience to physics to politics.  Its application to different fields has been the basis for a lot of academic work.

It was also responsible for a major series of political movements, one being the American Revolution.  The US constitution was written by scholars who knew the philosophies of Locke and Rousseau.  And these ideas were ultimately embodied in Darwinism and Adam Smith's capitalism as well.  They believed in allowing competition.  In a monarchy there was no competition.  In a collection of states, each was an experiment that generates different ways of life.  And, the good ideas would become self-evident and the federal government could then select the most effective laws and policies.

The late 1800's and early 1900's saw some fundamental shifts in the world that forced people to think differently and some philosophical groundwork that influenced the theory of relativity was laid through Marx via Veblen, who Einstein explicitly references.  However while relativity itself bears almost no resemblance to darwinian philosophy, the basic philosophical concept that preceded it was in some ways based on that.

One could say that some of the theory behind relativity is just an acknowledgement that the selection force of evolutionary theory is arbitrary and based on perspective.

Relativity exhibits this basic idea by showing that there is no inherently objective or privileged point of observation and that the very laws of physics seem to vary by vantage point, let alone things such as assigned utility of certain commodities.

Marxism displays this in discussing that those who gain capital do so by aligning their behavior with those who have assets.  They then can use that capital to encourage others to align their behavior with their own behavior, which allows them to gain more assets.  In this way the "selection factor" of the capitalist invisible hand is determined not by some inherent utility or degree of fitness but by the initial distribution of capital.  So in a society where every drives a car, people demand cars.  This is not because cars inherently have more utility than public transportation, but because the environment itself is shaped by the fact that people already use cars.

Part of this could be attributed to the fact that cultures suddenly were coming into more contact with each others.  Before the railroad, it was unlikely for the average person to come into contact with people of a totally different culture.  Perhaps there were slight differences, but most of the basics were the same for most people.  But in the late 1800's and early 1900's suddenly there was a markedly increased speed of communication and transportation.

The political face of this philosophy was communism.  However it was riddled with problems.  But why?  The theoretical foundation has produced great progress in many fields.

The problem is that while in the world there is no privileged perspective, you cannot occupy more than one of them at a time.  Ultimately, a frame of reference needs to be chosen, and the one that usually gets chosen is based on convenience.

When the Soviets removed the Tsars in Russia, they knew that those who control the means of production have the power, and they wanted that power to go to the workers. But nobody had determined how the workers were supposed to collectively control the means of production.  So the system of sharing control ultimately defaulted to back-up systems that were already in place--familial relationships, base power struggles, etc.

But most of the elements of capitalism are self-organizing systems that had already developed on top of those methods of sharing control.  They developed as a progression from those, with some deliberate philosophical design as well as some bottom-up tendencies.  To most feudal peasants--though I don't say all--capitalism would probably look pretty decent.

While this modern, relativistic style of thinking adds a lot of good theoretical information, it lacks some realism.  The fact that there is no privileged point of view is true, but we are always working with some point of view.  The ideal would be to pick the right point of view at the right time.  But that point of view has to be selected somehow.

Modern liberal ideology also developed from some of these same ideas and tries to include all perspectives but doesn't provide any means for those perspectives to successfully interact with each other.  The result is that all attempts at collective action default to using the already existing means of interaction.  This means that when disparate movements like the civil rights movement, or feminism, or gay rights interact with each other, they use either a) our hegemonic capitalism by giving capital support, which is gained through employment which is moderated through our political system which is dedicated to preserving the cheapest possible labor force, or b) the most primary means of organization like protests or small community brainstorming sessions which were rendered ineffectual long ago by that political and economy hegemony.
