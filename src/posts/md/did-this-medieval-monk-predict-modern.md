OK the title is clickbait, but I was watching [this video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Hg0c32KbA-g&ab_channel=Let%27sTalkReligion) and thought there were some interesting parallels between what Eckhart had to say about the trinity, some things in the [Tao Te Ching](https://www.yellowbridge.com/onlinelit/daodejing01.php), and what we have recently started to learn about the physics of dissipative systems and emergence.

What Eckhart says in the video is a sort of mystical explanation for how god is related to the trinity. He describes a “ground” which we all share with God, which is appears to be some kind of void. This ground is excited and sort of creates a “boiling”. He distinguishes between and internal and external boiling.

This is surprisingly reminiscent of how the Tao is described in the first chapter of the Tao Te Ching. Since I’m not an expert in Chinese, I can only go by the other translations. But there appears to be some alignment. Here’s my interpretation of the first chapter in Ekchart’s terms: “(1) The God that can be percieved is not the ultimate God (2) The holy spirit is the origin of everything . Through god the father everything else emerges. (3) We can only perceive the holy spirit if we are without desire. God and the holy spirit are the same, but with different representations, it behaves differently. (4) God with and without representations makes up the whole mysterious universe, and spirituality is about exploring where that mystery is deepest”. The mapping isn’t 100% perfect, but to me there’s clearly some overlap.

Something that’s interesting is this concept of the named/unnamed Tao, as well as this inner boiling and “boiling over”. To me this line represents something described in Goedel Escher Bach. Before a symbolic system reaches a certain level of complexity, it can be fully self-consistent. We could say it’s pure or perfect—God-like. But beyond a certain level of complexity, it starts to develop contradictions. In particular, this line is the line of being able to self-reference… or to “name” itself.

This is weirdly reminiscent of the concept of a dissipative system, or "far from equilibrium" system. From a pool of indistinguished matter or spacetime, when energy continues to flow through it, eventually there is an introduction of an excited state in contrast to the equilibrium state. There's [this paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09090) that came out relatively recently talking about how emergence in physics has computational properties. In other words, from nothing and an excited state, there’s a sort of boiling. A sort of symbolic system can emerge in the boiling. Terrence Deacon might call this realm “Morphodynamics” and in fact convection cells in a boiling pot of water is a specific example cited as representative of that concept. I believe category theory can explain how these relationships start to compound on each other, creating more types of relationships, more types of states, etc. Category theory is also the basis of functional programming languages. So the description of God as "pure intellect" sort of rings in there... perhaps one way god manifests is in the pure logical rules, which eventually give way to mystery again.

Sabine Hossenfelder also made [this video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AFmBq1Hg9os&ab_channel=SabineHossenfelder) on a paper that came out recently which proved that through that emergence, there are potentially dynamics which are impossible to calculate, even if we know all the rules governing their emergence. So there can be a sort of true stratification that arises between the foundation, and the elements which arise from that foundation further downstream. So when the boiling reaches a certain point, this inner boiling “boils over” into a symbolic system which can self-reference. This is what Deacon would call “Telodynamics”. It is then Turing complete and can represent any other thing. But it is logically inconsistent. It’s not possible to have access directly to the ground through the symbolic system.
