Any system has a certain rate of decay. The components of a system are probabilistically related to each other, so there’s a chain of probabilities of error. If we consider a system as a causal dependency graph, then we can say that each link of the chain has the potential for failure. Some systems devote a certain amount of resources to redundancies or error-checking. We can consider noisy channel codes to understand how much effort is required to reduce the error rate by a certain amount.

This gives us a model where we consider each event acting on the system as a monoidal action. At each time T, either the identity action can occur, or some other action which has some effect on the system. If we consider the adjacency matrix of the dependency graph, we can consider any state change to be some non-identity operation on that graph.

In order for a system to maintain stability or grow, it needs to constrain parts of its environment to convert some of that environment’s energy into potential energy that becomes part of the system. In our adjacency matrix this would either be modeled by expanding the matrix to include the new causal dependencies that have entered the system, or by expanding the system we’re looking at to include the environment. This must happen at a rate faster than the natural tendency to disintegrate given the instability of the system.

Consider a plant. It fixes carbon atoms by constraining these molecules using light energy. The carbon chain it created then becomes a part of the plant itself. The dependency configuration they bring includes some cycles. A carbon atom in a cellulose chain may push against other carbons, and those in turn push back on it. The constraint that the system places on that carbon atom in order to integrate it also can be accounted for as cyclical dependencies.

In order for the plant to do this, from the perspective of the plant, it must be able to identify free energy in the environment. From the plant’s perspective, it must have information about the environment. In other words, it must have some mechanism which can cause work to be done in such a way as to constrain some part of the plant’s system. That constraint takes the form of potential energy or dependency cycles. So the environment must be able to trigger some new cyclical dependency in the plant.

When the plant grows through photosynthesis, this is not normally associated with “observation”. It’s not clear that this in any way increases the ability of the plant to adjust and anticipate future environmental states. However it does increase the mass of the plant. If we consider the planck relation, we could say that it increases the wavelength or decreases the frequency of the plant. In other words, the entire system is resonating at a lower frequency. Other resonances of various sub components have a higher frequency because they are smaller can then be arranged at a phase within a cycle at that lower frequency. Changes in that phase can be physically identified and tracked.

In order to survive and grow, that information must be an accurate reflection of reality.
