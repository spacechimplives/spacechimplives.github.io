For instance: do humans correspond to cells?  Do institutional policies, laws, business processes or other institutional "codes" correlate to DNA?  How does money fit into the picture?

Cells are living organisms in themselves which, due to interdependence and specialization so not have the functionality to be fully independent.  In the realm of institutions, if there is a correlate to cells, I think this most clearly would correspond to a sub-institution.  In a case such as a kingdom with sub-kingdoms, the sub-kingdom will tend to retain the functionality to maintain its independence, complete with decision-making processes, etc.

Cells are the smallest unit of biological life, and their membranes provide the boundary between the inside and outside.  However institutions are not constrained to a given space and time in the same way that biological life appears to be, so there may not be a static correlate of a cell in institutional life.  The boundary may be maintained more globally.  For an example in the biological world, we could look at ant colonies, which similarly are distributed.  They enforce the boundary through pheromones which allow them to identify members from outside their colony, as well as the physical structures they build.

It's then tempting to consider the whole colony to be like a cells, which would bring us back to viewing whole corporations as cells, which would mean that sub-organizations like divisions and departments are more like organelles than cells.  An institutional "cell" would need to enforce a physical boundary.  If such a construct doesn't exist in the institution realm, then the distinction between cell and tissue probably doesn't either.

Another way is to identify analogous structures and try to work from there.  One thing that's true in biological life is that DNA has a special place within cells--the nucleus.  It's the most protected part of the cell, with multiple layers of defense between it and the outside world, and yet it is also ultimately responsible for directing the action of the cell.  In the case of human institutions, the equivalent seems to be government.  In ancient city-states we see a similar pattern to a cell where there's an outer barrier (city wall), as well as an inner barrier (palace grounds).  A religion has a barrier between internal and external (profession of belief), as well as an internal barrier (priesthood).  Their temples also tend to have a second internal barrier protecting the scripture--the tabernacle in Christianity.  Corporations as well have tended to have a distinction between management and labor.  So in institutions, we have a tendency to have a barrier separating the decision-making apparatus from the entities executing other functions.  This barrier not only provides physical protection, but likely enforces a sort of decoupling.

So if we say that our code of law, morals and business processes are analogous to DNA, then we should consider what might be analogous to transcription to mRNA. Transcription is the first step in the process of translating the code into something which has tangible effects.  In the case of chemistry it’s a variant of code which gets translated into mRNA.  Our code is stored in bits or letters.  It is then compiled to machine code, or it is translated to documents which hold a certain discrete value, for instance a diploma, birth certificate, or apostille.
