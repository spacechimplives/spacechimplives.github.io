Any social bonds whatsoever constrain choice to some extent. The question is whether that constraint allows a higher level of choice in the long run. For instance paying taxes constrains choice, but then in lots of contexts having a library built from the taxes creates more choices than the extra cash in your pocket would have created in a world without libraries. The total gain in choice has to be larger than the choice sacrificed by compliance for it to be worth a person's while to comply.

In other words the total degrees of freedom must be higher than you started with for the interaction to be an equilibrium.  In other words the exchange of energy (dollars) must increase the entropy to create a stable equilibrium.  Otherwise the bond will either not form, or it will be broken.

This is just the same as molecules forming from atoms.  New degrees of freedom open up when the atoms are bound together, despite the fact that each individual atom is more constrained than before.  In a way each new degree of freedom is like new territory opening up that these atoms can now move into.  If you can give up 1 acre to gain 20 acres shared with 5 other people, it seems like that would make sense, unless you're convinced they will be bad people to share with.

Quantifying the increase in choice due to a library versus the decrease in choice due to paying higher taxes is tough, though.  Ultimately it is a guess until a system is developed to quantify such a return.  And still, such an arrangement is subject to tragedy of the commons constraints unless the bonds are somehow enforced.
