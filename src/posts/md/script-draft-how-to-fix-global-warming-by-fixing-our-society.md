There's a lot of doom and gloom these days about global warming especially, but many problems in general that are facing our society.  Our society seems to be in freefall collapse.  I mean seriously, the children are killing each other.  Mass shootings have become routine.  That should be setting off alarm bells.  If there were any human beings at the helm of this ship, they would be rapidly trying to change course.  

Unfortunately the problems the world is facing have stacked up quickly in the last few years, so that global warming is no longer even the focus of our attention, even as it quickly becomes more serious. 

Anyway, we all know about these problems.  I will go ahead and give my suggestions for a solution and then explain how I arrived there.  The ultimate goal of my solution is to design a new small-scale social structure that can compete with capitalism on its own terms.  Ultimately the employer-employee relationship needs to be replaced by something that is more flexible, and allows more even distributions of power, perhaps not exactly partnership, but something closer to it.  In the meantime until that is designed and implemented, the solution is to increase partnership, as well as bottom-up information transfer in all of our relationships, and 3rd, specific tools which can help with both of those and aid the design of that alternative social structure.

For #1, more partnership means shared equity.  Of course everyone can't get equity in their jobs, but if you have the choice, then value that equity.  If you're a freelancer, try to partner up with people.  It's very difficult to be partners... there are constant conflicting interests & prisoner's dilemma situations, and it requires mutual compromise.  I'm not suggesting anyone get suckered into an uneven partnership, but if you have the opportunity to enter a partnership, experiment with it.  If you have the option, try to make tools for it, break new ground in technology for mediating partnerships.  Share your experiences... the good and the bad.  If you have the option to cooperate instead of compete, give it a shot.  

For #2, what I mean is to simply move more information up the hierarchy. For example, if you're supervising people, when making decisions: get as much information from the people you're supervising, even if very informally, as you can.  If you're being supervised, poll your coworkers and bring that information to your supervisor.  If you have the opportunity, build tools for mediating these kinds of decisions, and if you have the opportunity to try those tools out, do it.  The more quantitative this information can be, the better. 

So admittedly, these seem like very innocuous actions that will never have an impact.  However, I believe this is the only likely path to solving global warming in the next decade.  Here's why.

Let's list off the alternatives:  

a. pressure governments
b. invest in green technology
c. revolution
d. recycling, etc.

First, we can no longer rely on governments.  No matter how much petitioning we do, they will not act in our interest any longer.   Why?  Because corporations control massively more wealth than they do.  This shift really took hold in the 70s and 80s, and we can all see the effects.  Whatever work governments do, corporations can undo more quickly.  If a government decides not to play ball, the corporations will a) prop up opposition within that country, or b) move to another country.  Stop calling your national senators.  It's not the kind of upward information transfer that is meaningful.

Second, revolution will not do anything positive.  Any serious revolution would be a war that takes decades.  That will completely distract from global warming.  It will result in more emissions, and at the end of it all, we will still be trapped in the same cycle that we're in now

Investing in green energy sounds nice but without addressing the structural problems, the roll-out will never speed up.  The fossil-fuel companies will always look like the better investment, because with investment, the best indicator of future profits is current profits.  Those same fossil-fuel companies will use that investment to increase barriers for individuals to access those technologies, which will continue to make them look like a better investment. 

And recycling is just a game theory problem.  By spending your time thinking about recycling, you're reducing the amount of time you could be thinking about your job or your family, and your city is probably just throwing it in the trash anyway.  Without mass adoption, it's just not effective, and it will never have mass adoption without a real incentive.
