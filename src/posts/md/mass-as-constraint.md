In a previous [discussion about constraints and action](https://spacechimplives.substack.com/p/constraints-entropy-and-action), I referenced the idea that mass could be modeled as a set of constraints on infinitesimal masses. Those masses themselves can be broken up into constrained particles as well.

The basic idea was that by treating masses as groups of mutually constrained particles, we create a symmetry which reduces the size of the phase space by collapsing the distributions of all those particles into a single one.

In doing so we establish an energy value for a certain phase space. By “deleting” it and collapsing it into the mass of another particle, as we increment the mass, we’re increasing the energy in the system of the one combined particle, while “removing” all the energy of the system which we are collapsing. The total energy of the system which is getting collapsed into the single particle system through constraint is then accounted for in the mass of that system.

However we established in the previous discussions that potential energy is equivalent to constraint. If that’s the case, then we should be able to derive a concept of mass that doesn’t involve collapsing infinitesimally particles into each other, but simply the constraints themselves. Constraints appear to be essentially operations on a phase space which reduce its total volume.

So how could mass emerge from those operations themselves? Let’s first think about the breaking of symmetry which unfolds new states and creates the phase space to begin with. In doing this, we move from a state of full symmetry to a single broken symmetry which creates 1 bit. That creates a phase space in which 2 states can be occupied. ChatGPT suggests this is an information theoretical parallel to the Higgs mechanism which involves spontaneous symmetry breaking. I’m a bit skeptical, but I’m pasting it at the bottom in case anyone knows more about it than I do and can verify whether it’s worth exploring more. Regardless, we can create those 2 states of equal energy, we also then have to consider the energy of those states.
