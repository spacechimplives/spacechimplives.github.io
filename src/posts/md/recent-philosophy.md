In the last post, I mentioned several recent academic trends, from the sociological-type modeling that is being done with game theory lately, biopower that is discussed in anthropology, as well as the far from equilibrium studies in physics, social cognition in cognitive science, and recent developments in epigenetics.

So is there any overarching philosophy that can be taken from these disparate fields that differs materially from the previous philosophies that can be used for the basis of a political system that doesn't devolve into dictatorship?

That's a pretty leading question to ask.  Obviously I think the answer is yes or I wouldn't have been so specific.

I think these all paint the picture of multiple kinds of parts bonding in decentralized ways to create a stable, larger overall structure that then is hierarchically superior to those parts, catalyzes those parts, and replaces them with the newly catalyzed versions. This, opposed to one crystal of all the same type, or one central seed controlling many separate parts, or complexity forming from parts which already exist in large concentrations.

Much of this is discussed to some extent in several books which I can cite more thoroughly later, but *Mind in Life, Dynamics in Action, *and*, Incomplete Nature* (which is accused of plagiarizing from the first two, but is the one I have read all the way through..).  They concentrate more on the biochemical aspects, and not on the sociological parallels I hope to draw.

These concepts are not counter-intuitive.  In fact they are very intuitive, it is just that people have finally focused their efforts on them, and there are enough parallels in various fields to finally put a lot of these pieces together.
