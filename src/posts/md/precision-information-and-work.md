Locating a certain object relative to another can be considered equivalent the creation of a mutual constraint. As described previously in (this essay), we can see that those constraints are a form of internal energy.

This energy must come from somewhere. This means that if we can calculate the energy created through the constraint, we can calculate the minimum energy required to achieve a certain level of precision.

Imagine we are in a single dimension and we identify that an object is to our left. It may be moving relative to us, it may not be. With no other information we cannot know where it will be next. In locating the object to our left, we change the probability density function of its location. We double the probability of it being on the left, and send the probability of it being on the right to 0. In doing so we create precisely 1 bit of information.

After that initial sample determining that the object is to our left, the probability density function will move back to a uniform distribution. The speed at which the asymmetry will dissolve is related to the temperature. If we again make an observation, we can again constrain the probability density function to one half of the dimension, and thus again add 1 bit of information. This sampling process illustrates a message channel whose channel capacity we can measure. And if each sample requires a certain amount of energy to perform, then we can say that maintaining that 1 bit of information requires a certain amount of energy to maintain over a certain period of time.

In this case the sampling is used to maintain information about the location, however another way to maintain that information is via constraint. By creating some bond or constraint to the object so that we can predict where it will be, we can similarly maintain that information.

On some level, these 2 situations are equivalent. A certain amount of energy goes into creating and maintaining a bond over a given period of time. There will be perturbations which the bond will have to withstand. Eventually that bond will fail with some probability. By sampling the object consistently, the system which is sampling can maintain information about the object in question. Some of those samples will be inaccurate. Eventually a finite amount of energy will run out and no more samples will be taken, at which point the probability distribution falls back to a uniform distribution, increasing entropy.

The creation of 1 bit of information means the reduction of entropy by 1 bit as well, which means the conversion of some amount of entropic energy into free energy. The amount of that energy depends on what the probability density function is modeling… that is, what is included as a state.
