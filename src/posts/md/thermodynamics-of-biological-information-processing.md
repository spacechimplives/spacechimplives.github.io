In the body, when we are processing information, we take sensory inputs which provide many samples.  These samples come over a noisy channel.  When we're throwing a ball at a target, we don't know exactly how far away it is.  We guess based on stereoscopic cues as well as others.  These cues can be wrong as optical illusions show.  We can view this as a communication over a noisy channel.

We integrate these samples into representations that are more and more abstract.  These more abstract representations may be stored in a distributed way among many neurons. They change less over time.  They have many inputs but fewer outputs.  They are essentially performing the function of abstraction.  What is abstraction?  It is essentially pulling out the most important bits of information and discarding the rest. When you discard those bits, you lose the ability to distinguish those details.  That information has become entropy.  An agent will no longer be able to use it to find the optimum path.  But with enough other cues, the energy saved by not storing the bits might be worth it. In a way this is like a lossy compression.

In the brain, these abstract representations are transformed and combined through many different processes.  They can be abstracted further, they can be combined with new abstractions coming in from new inputs.  These combinations of abstractions can themselves be pared down by discarding some of the information.  Not only that, but the brain isn't as precise as a computer and is more susceptible to hardware errors.  There may be an unknown number of steps that can each be modeled as noisy communications followed by lossy compression.
