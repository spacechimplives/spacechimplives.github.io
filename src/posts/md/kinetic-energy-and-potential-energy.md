If a system inhabits only a single state of many potential states, we can say that it has maximal information or free energy. At the same time, we must wonder what’s preventing it from inhabiting other states.

The temperature for such a system is undefined. We could interpret that as no temperature in the system, so there is no impetus for the system to change states. Or we could say that the internal forces of the system are carefully balanced so the system is mutually constrained into what we’ve labelled as a single state. These are equivalent for the purposes of our system because the potential energy present in the carefully balanced forces are part of what make up the internal energy of the system.

Now let’s halve the mass and give it access to another state. We can let the new halved mass randomly oscillates between these 2 states.

Suddenly we’ve added a (literal) bit of entropy. Thus there’s an entropic energy term added to the system (delta S \* Temperature). But we can also read this as the addition of kinetic energy, because the mass will move between 2 states. When we know which state the system is in and which state it will move to, we have kinetic energy, which is sometimes accounted for as a non-entropic term of the system. We have 2 distinguishable states which are not lumped together into a single macrostate. When we don’t know which state it’s in, it’s accounted for as entropic energy. We have a single macrostate which contains 2 microstates.

Now let’s start again and divide the mass again as we add more states. If we carefully tune the number of states we add at each step, we can increase the entropic energy at the same rate that we’re decreasing the internal energy or rest energy due to the mass. At the far end of that limit, we have a infinitely small particle zooming around at the speed of light. There is no internal or rest energy term. There is only kinetic or entropic energy. We know that we cannot detect infinitely small particles, only quantized, so we should expect to observe quantization effects as well in the amount of entropy we measure.

We can achieve similar results by decreasing the size of the mass while speeding it up to achieve a relativistic increase in effective mass. As the mass approaches infinitely fast and infinitely small, we lose the rest energy term, but gain either a kinetic energy term or an entropic energy term. Ultimately this corresponds to a “massless” photon with kinetic energy.

Let’s take a bunch of these photons with energy such that they add up to the same rest mass we started with. Let’s take 2 of those photons and attach them by a massless rod. Suddenly from their perspective, they’re not moving relative to each other. In other words, their kinetic energy relative to each other is 0. They also have reduced each other’s access to states in the system. The number of states accessible to particle B give a certain state of particle A has been drastically reduced, which reduces the entropy of the system.

Let’s bind all of these particles to each other in a similar manner. Now we have one giant mass and we’ve constrained all the conditional probabilities of these particles’ relative positions and momenta into 1 state. The mass still can move around from our perspective, so let’s further attach a few rods from this bunch of particles to our own observation device, so that the overarching system has 0 kinetic energy. There is now only 1 state this system can exhibit.

The specific way we’ve removed states from the system is through the addition of symmetries. As we add symmetries, we reduce the number of distinguishable states the system can reach by reducing the number of distinguishable states, period. This reduces accessible phase space volume on the surface because we don’t account for these states which have been deleted from the system. If we did, mass would count toward the entropic energy.

Often that symmetry manifests as forces balanced against each other to create potential energy. The opposing forces mutually constrain each other and create a potential. When we go the other way, we “unbalance” the elements making up the system, and that results in both forces being released as kinetic energy.

They technically may be in different positions, and we know there’s a theoretical phase space volume that they could inhabit, but they have 1 effective state. The system is now equivalent to the original. There is now 0 kinetic energy. There is the original rest mass. All we did is constrain each particle in the system, and with each constraint we reduced the accessible phase space volume. So what we can say is that there’s a direct relationship between the potential energy, the kinetic energy, and the accessible phase space volume.

We might even say that the action \*is\* the cumulative sum of accessible phase space volume. Meaning the Lagrangian represents the rate of change accessible phase space volume. The action is an measure of phase space volume. Quantized, that’s a number of accessible states. We could create a parallel equation which takes the integral of entropy minus “energy of constraint” or internal energy. This would allow us to apply the principle of least action to information theoretical systems.
