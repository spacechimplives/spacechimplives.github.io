What can we do?  We can design a new system, and determine a way to transition to that system.   Any violent revolution is a non-starter.  It just leaves a power vacuum where a less evolved structure will fill the void.  There's no way anyone's going to be paying attention to global warming if there's a war going on, sorry LARPers.

 

If we're designing a new system, what approach should we take?  If we were going to be 100% rigorous, first we need a way to define relevant system components unambiguously.  The we need to describe our current system in terms of those components.  From there, we need to identify what caused the previous system to fail, and then we need to come up with new configurations of those components and analyze how they would behave with different inputs.  This level of rigor requires some resources

that ultimately redirect the energy of any groups which want significant change against each other.  For any radical group, there is another which opposes it.  This is a good thing in a system which is on track, but for one which isn't, it prevents change.

So what can we do about the waning effectiveness of these systems?  To me it seems clear that we're in a situation where everyone is locked into a set of intertwined incentives that reinforce themselves and have created a positive feedback loop.  The more we follow those incentives, the more powerful they are, the less capable of change from within the system is.  Even the "powerful" people within these systems don't seem very happy with them.  The lives of our politicians seem hollow, but what else can they do?  If they vote against whatever billionaire-funded policy their party is pushing, they will be blasted by the media and the party will throw its entire weight against them in the primary.

And then you can blame the billionaires funding those policies, but if they didn't take the opportunity that arose because their empathy kicked in, then someone who lacked the empathy to refuse the opportunity would have, and they would be the billionaire instead.  Weaknesses in our systems get exploited.  Relying on empathy or compassion to motivate people not to exploit our system will consume them and ultimately they will run out.  Don't get me wrong: we can and should use empathy and compassion to inform the design of our systems, but we should use money & enforcement policies to motivate people to adhere to those systems, not human kindness.

So what is powering these incentives?  People point to money as the obvious choice, but it's not the incentive so much as the medium.  Like a neurotransmitter carrying a nerve impulse.  It is a sort of representation of all our other motivators.  A single mother trying to feed & clothe her kids, a nerd with an inferiority complex trying to prove his worth, a religious zealot trying to convert the world, an artist trying to find the time to express themselves --- money helps them all in their cause.  So our incentives are determined by a) how we can make money and b) how we can spend money.  Changing either of those is changing our incentives, and can therefore guide our actions.
