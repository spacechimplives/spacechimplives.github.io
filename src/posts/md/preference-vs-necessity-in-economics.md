$$ \frac{MU_{xn}}{MU_{yn}} = \frac{P_x}{P_y} = \frac{MPP_{ay}}{MPP_{ax}} $$

First off, to summarize what it means-- it's defining the relationship at equilibrium between the price, the utility of a good to a consumer (demand), and the efficiency at which it can be produced at a given volume (supply).  It's illustrating that if consumers are deciding whether they want to spend their money on x or on y, they will allocate their money according to the marginal utility they're expecting to get.  Similarly if a producer is deciding what to produce with their "production factor", they will choose according to which gives the best price relative to the amount of production factor it uses.

These equations alone don't give us our supply and demand curve.  Instead what we do is exchange one of these goods for dollars.  Now if each consumer substitutes dollars in for one of the products in the first term, and if the supplier substitutes dollars in for one of the product factors, then we arrive at a decision about whether they would buy or sell a given product, and in doing so puts them on the supply or demand curve respectively.

In these equations, both the concept of preference and necessity are included in "utility."  As a consumer chooses between different products and compares them, if they really want a certain food, but another one gives them an essential nutrient, then if their necessity for that nutrient is high enough it will overpower their preference for the first food.  If they have plenty of that nutrient, then perhaps their preference will win over.  This makes sense.

Yet when two separate consumers approach the market, their dollars are each worth the same amount to the vendor.  The internal comparison among their choices does not contribute total demand.  The seller doesn't really mind if the dollars come from person A or person B, a dollar is a dollar.  Whether the person is willing to pay a certain price out of preference or necessity is not material for the purposes of such a market.  So we put those two people one the same curve for the demand.

This is all fine and good when we’re looking at a single market, but it begins to matter when we try to create a macroeconomic model considering many markets and summing quantities over them.
